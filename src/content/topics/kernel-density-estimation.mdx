## 30.1 Motivation: from ECDF to density

Topic 29 built its entire nonparametric toolkit on the empirical CDF $F_n(x) = n^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}\{X_i \le x\}$ — a step function that converges uniformly to the true CDF $F$. But $F_n$ is *not* differentiable, so the density $f = F'$ is unreachable by naïve differentiation. Kernel density estimation is the answer to the obvious question: *what happens when we differentiate $F_n$ using a smooth weighting function instead of a Dirac delta?*

The idea is simple enough to state in a sentence. Replace each sample point $X_i$ with a small bump of area $1/n$ centered at $X_i$; add the bumps up. The sum is a bona fide density (nonnegative, integrates to 1) that *looks like* $f$ as $n$ grows, with a smoothness controlled by the bump width. Histograms are the roughest version — rectangular bumps of constant width, anchored to arbitrary bin edges. KDE replaces those rectangles with a smooth, symmetric, nonnegative kernel and drops the bin-edge arbitrariness entirely.

<ExampleBlock number={1} title="Histograms and KDEs on the same N(0,1) sample">
Figure 1 takes a single Normal$(0, 1)$ sample of size $n = 200$ and plots three histograms (bin widths $b \in \{0.1, 0.4, 1.0\}$) alongside three Gaussian-kernel KDEs (bandwidths $h \in \{0.08, 0.3, 0.8\}$). At small $b$ or small $h$, both estimators are spiky and variance-dominated; at large $b$ or large $h$, both over-smooth and bias-dominate. The KDEs remove one obvious source of arbitrariness — bin edges — but the bandwidth problem remains, just in a smoother form.
</ExampleBlock>

Bottom row: three Gaussian-KDE curves of the same sample at bandwidths 0.08, 0.3, and 0.8. *Figure 1. Histograms (top) and KDEs (bottom) on the same $n = 200$ Normal$(0, 1)$ sample. The bin-width / bandwidth trade-off between spikiness and over-smoothing is visible in both rows; KDE removes the bin-edge arbitrariness but keeps the smoothing-parameter choice.*

<RemarkBlock number={1} title="What KDE gives up vs. histograms">
Histograms have two genuine advantages KDEs lack: they are exactly piecewise-constant (so integrals over bin intervals are trivial) and they are visually honest about the underlying empirical counts. KDEs are smoother and easier to differentiate further (for mode-finding or density-gradient methods), but the smoothness is *imposed*, not *inherent*. A reader who wants to know "how many data points fell in $[a, b]$?" should look at a histogram; a reader who wants a differentiable density estimate should look at a KDE.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={2} title="Distribution-free density estimation">
Topic 7 built density families (Normal, Gamma, Beta) and fit parametric $\theta$ from data. That program is efficient when the family is right and biased when it's wrong. KDE drops the family assumption and estimates $f$ directly from the sample — a distribution-free procedure in the sense of Topic 29 §29.1 Rem 1. The price is a slower convergence rate: we'll see in §30.6 that AMISE decays at $n^{-4/5}$, slower than the parametric $n^{-1}$, and that this is the price of distribution-freeness.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.2 The kernel density estimator

With the motivation out of the way, we can write down the estimator formally and check that it is a density. Three definitions (kernel, scaled kernel, KDE) and one stated theorem (KDE is a valid density) — enough to set up everything that follows.

<DefinitionBlock number={1} title="Kernel">
A **kernel** is a function $K : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ satisfying

$$
\int_{-\infty}^\infty K(u)\,du = 1, \qquad K(-u) = K(u) \text{ for all } u.
$$

Nonnegativity and unit mass make $K$ a density on $\mathbb{R}$; symmetry makes it a density centered at $0$. We additionally require finite variance $\mu_2(K) := \int u^2 K(u)\,du < \infty$ and finite roughness $R(K) := \int K(u)^2\,du < \infty$, both used throughout §30.4–§30.6.
</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={2} title="Scaled kernel">
For bandwidth $h > 0$, the **scaled kernel** is

$$
K_h(u) := \frac{1}{h}\, K\!\left(\frac{u}{h}\right).
$$

The scaling preserves the density property — $\int K_h(u)\,du = 1$ for any $h > 0$ — and turns $h$ into a width parameter: small $h$ makes $K_h$ tall and narrow (close to a Dirac delta); large $h$ makes it short and wide.
</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={3} title="Kernel density estimator (KDE)">
Given an iid sample $X_1, \dots, X_n$ from an unknown density $f$, the **kernel density estimator** with kernel $K$ and bandwidth $h$ is

$$
\hat f_h(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n K_h(x - X_i) \;=\; \frac{1}{n\,h}\sum_{i=1}^n K\!\left(\frac{x - X_i}{h}\right).
$$

Both forms are in active use. The first makes it visually obvious that $\hat f_h$ is an average of $n$ kernel-shaped bumps centered at the data. The second groups the bandwidth normalization into a single $nh$ factor, which will be convenient when we compute bias and variance in §30.4.
</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={1} title="The KDE is a valid density">
Under the definitions above, $\hat f_h$ is a bona-fide probability density:

$$
\hat f_h(x) \ge 0 \text{ for all } x, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^\infty \hat f_h(x)\,dx = 1.
$$

*Stated; the proof is elementary.* Nonnegativity is immediate from $K \ge 0$. Integration reduces to $\frac{1}{n}\sum_i \int K_h(x - X_i)\,dx = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i 1 = 1$ by the scaled-kernel normalization.
</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={2} title="A hand-computed KDE at two points">
Take $n = 2$ observations $X_1 = 0$, $X_2 = 1$ and Gaussian kernel $K(u) = \varphi(u) = (2\pi)^{-1/2} e^{-u^2/2}$. At bandwidth $h = 0.5$:

$$
\hat f_{0.5}(0.5) = \frac{1}{2 \cdot 0.5}\bigl[\varphi(1) + \varphi(-1)\bigr] = \varphi(1) + \varphi(-1) \approx 0.4839,
$$

where we used $\varphi(1) = \varphi(-1) \approx 0.2420$. The KDE at the midpoint $x = 0.5$ receives equal contribution from both observations and is therefore twice $\varphi(1)/h$. This is the T30.8 test pin — a two-point sanity check on the `kdeEvaluate` implementation.
</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={3} title="Equivalent formulation via convolution">
Writing $\mathbb{P}_n = n^{-1} \sum_i \delta_{X_i}$ for the empirical distribution (a probability measure that places mass $1/n$ at each sample point), we have $\hat f_h = K_h \ast \mathbb{P}_n$. KDE is the convolution of a smooth kernel with the empirical distribution — equivalently, it is the empirical density against a smoothed observation model. This formulation is convenient in formalml where the convolution structure generalizes to multivariate bandwidths and non-iid samples.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={4} title="Why the symmetry and nonnegativity constraints">
Symmetry $K(-u) = K(u)$ makes the first-moment condition $\int u K(u)\,du = 0$ automatic, which simplifies the bias calculation in §30.4 (the $h f'(x)$ Taylor term drops out). Nonnegativity guarantees $\hat f_h \ge 0$, so the estimator is always a valid density. A fourth-order or higher-order kernel with $\mu_2(K) = 0$ can achieve faster bias rates (down to $O(h^4)$) but requires $K$ to take negative values, and then $\hat f_h$ may be negative in places — a practical complication we'll mention in §30.6 Rem 13 and defer to Serfling 1980 §1.11.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.3 Kernels: families and properties

Five kernels are standard — Gaussian, Epanechnikov, biweight (quartic), triangular, and uniform — and every modern implementation supports all five. The constants that govern AMISE ($\mu_2(K)$ and $R(K)$) are closed-form for each, and their *efficiency* relative to Epanechnikov sits between 0.93 and 1.00 across the whole family. Kernel choice, in practice, matters much less than bandwidth choice.

<TheoremBlock number={2} title="Standard kernels and their constants">
The five standard symmetric kernels, with their second moments and roughness:

| Kernel | $K(u)$ | Support | $\mu_2(K)$ | $R(K)$ | Efficiency |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gaussian | $\varphi(u) = (2\pi)^{-1/2} e^{-u^2/2}$ | $\mathbb{R}$ | $1$ | $\frac{1}{2\sqrt\pi} \approx 0.2821$ | $0.951$ |
| Epanechnikov | $\frac{3}{4}(1 - u^2)\mathbf{1}\{|u| \le 1\}$ | $[-1, 1]$ | $\frac{1}{5}$ | $\frac{3}{5}$ | $1.000$ |
| Biweight | $\frac{15}{16}(1 - u^2)^2\mathbf{1}\{|u| \le 1\}$ | $[-1, 1]$ | $\frac{1}{7}$ | $\frac{5}{7}$ | $0.994$ |
| Triangular | $(1 - |u|)\mathbf{1}\{|u| \le 1\}$ | $[-1, 1]$ | $\frac{1}{6}$ | $\frac{2}{3}$ | $0.986$ |
| Uniform | $\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{1}\{|u| \le 1\}$ | $[-1, 1]$ | $\frac{1}{3}$ | $\frac{1}{2}$ | $0.930$ |

*Stated.* All values are elementary integrals; Example 3 verifies Epanechnikov explicitly, and the `kernelProperties` export in `nonparametric.ts` §8 encodes all five (T30.5–T30.6).
</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={3} title="Epanechnikov moments by direct integration">
For $K_E(u) = \frac{3}{4}(1 - u^2)\mathbf{1}\{|u| \le 1\}$:

$$
\int_{-1}^1 K_E(u)\,du = \frac{3}{4}\int_{-1}^1 (1 - u^2)\,du = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{4}{3} = 1 \quad\checkmark,
$$

$$
\mu_2(K_E) = \int_{-1}^1 u^2 K_E(u)\,du = \frac{3}{4}\int_{-1}^1 (u^2 - u^4)\,du = \frac{3}{4}\left(\frac{2}{3} - \frac{2}{5}\right) = \frac{1}{5},
$$

$$
R(K_E) = \int_{-1}^1 K_E(u)^2\,du = \frac{9}{16}\int_{-1}^1 (1 - u^2)^2\,du = \frac{9}{16} \cdot \frac{16}{15} = \frac{3}{5}.
$$

The values $\mu_2(K_E) = 1/5$ and $R(K_E) = 3/5$ are load-bearing for Theorem 5's Epanechnikov optimality proof in §30.6.
</ExampleBlock>

The efficiencies in Theorem 2 all lie in $[0.93, 1.00]$. ## 30.4 Leading-order bias and variance

The kernel and bandwidth are in place. Next we compute the mean and variance of $\hat f_h(x)$ at a fixed $x$ and extract their leading-order behavior as $n \to \infty$, $h \to 0$. This is Rosenblatt 1956's original calculation; it sets up everything that follows.

<TheoremBlock number={3} title="Leading-order bias and variance of the KDE">
Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be iid with density $f$ having bounded, continuous second derivative $f''$ in a neighborhood of $x$ with $f(x) > 0$. Let $K$ be a symmetric kernel satisfying the moment conditions $\int K = 1$, $\int u K = 0$, $\mu_2(K) < \infty$, $R(K) < \infty$. As $n \to \infty$, $h \to 0$, and $nh \to \infty$:

$$
\mathrm{Bias}\bigl(\hat f_h(x)\bigr) = \frac{h^2}{2}\,\mu_2(K)\, f''(x) + o(h^2),
$$

$$
\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\hat f_h(x)\bigr) = \frac{f(x)\, R(K)}{n\,h} + o\!\left(\frac{1}{nh}\right).
$$

*Stated here; the derivation is Step (i) and Step (ii) of the §30.6 Theorem 4 proof. Both calculations are a second-order Taylor expansion of $f$ around $x$ combined with the kernel moment conditions.*
</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={4} title="Bias and variance at the peak of N(0,1)">
For $f = \varphi$ (standard Normal), $x = 0$, Gaussian kernel ($\mu_2 = 1$, $R = (2\sqrt\pi)^{-1}$), $h = 0.5$, $n = 200$:

$$
f''(0) = -\varphi(0) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \approx -0.399, \quad f(0) = \varphi(0) \approx 0.399,
$$

$$
\mathrm{Bias} \approx \frac{0.25}{2} \cdot 1 \cdot (-0.399) = -0.0499, \quad \mathrm{Var} \approx \frac{0.399 \cdot 0.2821}{200 \cdot 0.5} \approx 0.00113.
$$

The bias is roughly 13% of $f(0)$; the standard deviation is roughly 8%. The leading-order formulas predict this at large $n$; at $n = 200$ there is finite-$n$ slippage, but the orders of magnitude are right.
</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={6} title="Why the first-order bias term vanishes">
The Taylor expansion of $f(x - hu)$ around $x$ has a linear $-hu f'(x)$ term. When integrated against the symmetric kernel $K$, the linear term is $-h f'(x) \int u K(u)\,du = 0$. Symmetry of $K$ is doing work here — without it we'd pick up an $O(h)$ bias term, and the AMISE-optimal bandwidth in §30.6 would have a very different rate. This is also why asymmetric kernels (one-sided boundary kernels in §30.5) need separate treatment.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={7} title="Rosenblatt's no-free-lunch result">
Theorem 3 says bias is $O(h^2)$ and variance is $O((nh)^{-1})$ under standard regularity — the two rates are intrinsic to the kernel-smoothing program and cannot be both improved without changing the estimator class. A fourth-order kernel with $\mu_2(K) = 0$ can achieve $O(h^4)$ bias but potentially negative $\hat f_h$ (§30.2 Rem 4); local polynomial regression (Fan–Gijbels 1996; §30.5 Rem 9) trades one estimator structure for another. In the plain-KDE world with a symmetric nonnegative kernel, Rosenblatt 1956 is the ceiling.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.5 MISE, AMISE, and boundary behavior

Bias and variance at a single point $x$ are the building blocks. The relevant global loss — the one AMISE-optimal bandwidth is minimizing — integrates pointwise mean-squared error over the full domain. We derive this integrated form and then flag the one regime where Theorem 3 does *not* hold: the boundary of a bounded-support density.

The pointwise mean-squared error is $\mathrm{MSE}(x) = \mathrm{Bias}^2(x) + \mathrm{Var}(x)$. Integrating over $x$,

$$
\mathrm{MISE}(h) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\int\bigl(\hat f_h(x) - f(x)\bigr)^2\,dx \;=\; \int \mathrm{Bias}^2(x)\,dx + \int \mathrm{Var}(x)\,dx
$$

is the **mean integrated squared error**, the canonical global loss functional. The **asymptotic MISE** (AMISE) keeps only the leading-order terms from Theorem 3:

$$
\mathrm{AMISE}(h) \;=\; \frac{h^4\,\mu_2(K)^2\, R(f'')}{4} \;+\; \frac{R(K)}{n\,h},
$$

where $R(f'') := \int f''(x)^2\,dx$ is the **curvature integral** of $f$. The two terms are the integrated squared bias (the first) and the integrated variance (the second) — a log-log plot shows them as a line with slope $+4$ and a line with slope $-1$, whose sum has a unique minimum we'll compute in §30.6.

<ExampleBlock number={5} title="MISE vs AMISE at finite n">
For $f = \varphi$, Gaussian kernel, $n = 200$: the closed-form AMISE-optimal bandwidth (from §30.6 Thm 4) is $h^\ast \approx 0.3705$. At that $h$, AMISE $\approx 0.01025$. The exact MISE computed by numerical integration over the same grid is $\approx 0.01058$ — about 3% higher than AMISE. The gap is the $o(h^2)$ and $o(1/(nh))$ terms we dropped; at $n = 200$ they are nonzero but small, and they vanish as $n \to \infty$.
</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={8} title="Why boundary bias is not a small correction">
For a density with bounded support $[a, b]$ and a kernel with support $[-r, r]$ (or all of $\mathbb{R}$ with rapid decay), the KDE near $x = a$ averages over $X_i$ values in a window that extends below $a$. But no $X_i$ values *are* below $a$ — the sample is entirely on $[a, b]$. The KDE therefore under-estimates $f(a)$ and smooths probability mass into the forbidden region $(-\infty, a)$. At $x$ exactly at the boundary, the bias is $O(h)$ — worse than the interior $O(h^2)$ — and the AMISE analysis of §30.6 does not apply. Any production density estimator for bounded-support data needs a boundary correction.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={9} title="Reflection, local-linear, and the path to formalml">

Local polynomial is the modern standard and generalizes cleanly to the regression setting $E[Y \mid X]$; the full treatment is deferred to formalml `local-regression`. Topic 30 keeps things simple with reflection.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.6 Optimal bandwidth and Epanechnikov's theorem

Two theorems anchor the featured section. Theorem 4 derives the AMISE-optimal bandwidth from the decomposition in §30.5 — the full four-step proof is the load-bearing calculation of the topic. Theorem 5 picks the kernel that minimizes the resulting AMISE among symmetric nonnegative kernels with a fixed second moment — a short calculus-of-variations argument whose answer is Epanechnikov's $K_E$.

<TheoremBlock number={4} title="AMISE decomposition and optimal bandwidth (featured)">
Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be iid with density $f$ having bounded, continuous second derivative $f''$. Let $K$ be a symmetric kernel with $\int K = 1$, $\int u K = 0$, $\mu_2(K) \in (0, \infty)$, $R(K) < \infty$. As $n \to \infty$, $h \to 0$, $nh \to \infty$,

$$
\mathrm{AMISE}(h) \;=\; \frac{R(K)}{nh} + \frac{h^4\,\mu_2(K)^2\, R(f'')}{4},
$$

where $R(f'') := \int f''(x)^2\,dx$. The AMISE-optimal bandwidth is

$$
h^\ast \;=\; \left(\frac{R(K)}{n\,\mu_2(K)^2\, R(f'')}\right)^{1/5} \;=\; O(n^{-1/5}),
$$

with matching minimum AMISE

$$
\mathrm{AMISE}^\ast \;=\; \frac{5}{4}\,\bigl\{R(K)^4\, \mu_2(K)^2\, R(f'')\bigr\}^{1/5}\, n^{-4/5}.
$$
</TheoremBlock>

<ProofBlock>
The proof has four steps: (i) compute the bias via Taylor expansion of $f$; (ii) compute the variance via the iid-sum identity; (iii) integrate over $x$ to obtain AMISE; (iv) minimize in $h$.

**Step (i): Bias.** Substitute $u = (x - y)/h$ in the definition of the expected value:

$$
\mathbb{E}\bigl[\hat f_h(x)\bigr] = \int \frac{1}{h} K\!\left(\frac{x-y}{h}\right) f(y)\,dy = \int K(u)\, f(x - hu)\,du.
$$

Taylor-expand $f(x - hu)$ around $x$ to second order, using the regularity of $f''$:

$$
f(x - hu) = f(x) - hu\, f'(x) + \frac{(hu)^2}{2}\, f''(x) + o(h^2),
$$

where the remainder is $o(h^2)$ uniformly in $u$ on the support of $K$. Substituting into the expectation integral,

$$
\mathbb{E}\bigl[\hat f_h(x)\bigr] = f(x)\int K(u)\,du - h\, f'(x)\int u\, K(u)\,du + \frac{h^2}{2}\, f''(x)\int u^2\, K(u)\,du + o(h^2).
$$

Applying the kernel moment conditions (K1)–(K3):

$$
\mathbb{E}\bigl[\hat f_h(x)\bigr] = f(x) \cdot 1 - h\, f'(x) \cdot 0 + \frac{h^2}{2}\, f''(x)\, \mu_2(K) + o(h^2).
$$

Subtracting $f(x)$ yields the leading-order bias $\mathrm{Bias}(\hat f_h(x)) = \frac{h^2}{2}\,\mu_2(K)\, f''(x) + o(h^2)$.

**Step (ii): Variance.** Since $\hat f_h(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i Z_i$ where $Z_i := h^{-1} K((x-X_i)/h)$ are iid,

$$
\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\hat f_h(x)\bigr) = \frac{1}{n}\, \mathrm{Var}(Z_1) = \frac{1}{n}\!\left\{\mathbb{E}[Z_1^2] - \mathbb{E}[Z_1]^2\right\}.
$$

Compute the second moment of $Z_1$ by the same substitution:

$$
\mathbb{E}[Z_1^2] = \int \frac{1}{h^2}\, K\!\left(\frac{x-y}{h}\right)^{\!2} f(y)\,dy = \frac{1}{h}\int K(u)^2\, f(x - hu)\,du.
$$

Using $f(x - hu) = f(x) + O(h)$ (first-order Taylor),

$$
\mathbb{E}[Z_1^2] = \frac{1}{h}\, f(x) \int K(u)^2\,du + O(1) = \frac{f(x)\, R(K)}{h} + O(1).
$$

The first-moment-squared term is $\mathbb{E}[Z_1]^2 = (f(x) + O(h^2))^2 = f(x)^2 + O(h^2)$, which is $O(1)$ and therefore negligible compared to the $O(1/h)$ leading term. Combining:

$$
\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\hat f_h(x)\bigr) = \frac{1}{n}\!\left\{\frac{f(x)\, R(K)}{h} + O(1)\right\} = \frac{f(x)\, R(K)}{nh} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right).
$$

Under the regime $nh \to \infty$, the $O(1/n)$ term is $o(1/(nh))$.

**Step (iii): Integrate to AMISE.** The pointwise mean-squared error is $\mathrm{MSE}(x) = \mathrm{Bias}^2(x) + \mathrm{Var}(x)$. Integrating over $x$ and keeping only the leading terms,

$$
\mathrm{AMISE}(h) = \int \mathrm{Bias}^2(x)\,dx + \int \mathrm{Var}(x)\,dx.
$$

The integrated squared bias is

$$
\int \mathrm{Bias}^2(x)\,dx = \int \left(\frac{h^2}{2}\,\mu_2(K)\,f''(x)\right)^{\!2} dx = \frac{h^4\,\mu_2(K)^2}{4}\,R(f''),
$$

where $R(f'') := \int f''(x)^2\,dx$. The integrated variance is

$$
\int \mathrm{Var}(x)\,dx = \int \frac{f(x)\,R(K)}{nh}\,dx = \frac{R(K)}{nh}\int f(x)\,dx = \frac{R(K)}{nh},
$$

using $\int f = 1$. Summing,

$$
\mathrm{AMISE}(h) = \frac{R(K)}{nh} + \frac{h^4\,\mu_2(K)^2\,R(f'')}{4}.
$$

**Step (iv): Minimize in $h$.** Differentiate AMISE with respect to $h$:

$$
\frac{d\,\mathrm{AMISE}}{dh} = -\frac{R(K)}{n\,h^2} + h^3\,\mu_2(K)^2\,R(f'').
$$

Setting to zero and solving for $h$:

$$
h^5 = \frac{R(K)}{n\,\mu_2(K)^2\,R(f'')}, \qquad h^\ast = \left(\frac{R(K)}{n\,\mu_2(K)^2\,R(f'')}\right)^{\!1/5}.
$$

The second derivative

$$
\frac{d^2\,\mathrm{AMISE}}{dh^2} = \frac{2R(K)}{n\,h^3} + 3 h^2\,\mu_2(K)^2\,R(f'') > 0,
$$

confirms $h^\ast$ is the unique minimum. Substituting $h^\ast$ into AMISE: the first term evaluates to

$$
\frac{R(K)}{n\,h^\ast} = \frac{R(K)}{n}\left(\frac{n\,\mu_2(K)^2\,R(f'')}{R(K)}\right)^{\!1/5} = R(K)^{4/5}\, \mu_2(K)^{2/5}\, R(f'')^{1/5}\, n^{-4/5},
$$

and the second term evaluates to

$$
\frac{(h^\ast)^4\, \mu_2(K)^2\, R(f'')}{4} = \frac{1}{4}\, R(K)^{4/5}\, \mu_2(K)^{2/5}\, R(f'')^{1/5}\, n^{-4/5}.
$$

Their sum is $\mathrm{AMISE}^\ast = \frac{5}{4}\, R(K)^{4/5}\, \mu_2(K)^{2/5}\, R(f'')^{1/5}\, n^{-4/5} = \frac{5}{4}\bigl\{R(K)^4\, \mu_2(K)^2\, R(f'')\bigr\}^{1/5}\, n^{-4/5}$.

$\blacksquare$ — using kernel moment conditions (K1)–(K4), second-order Taylor expansion of $f$, iid-variance accounting, and elementary calculus (ROS1956; PAR1962; WAN1995 §2.5; SIL1986 §3.3).
</ProofBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={5} title="Epanechnikov's optimality theorem">
Consider the class of symmetric kernels $K : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ satisfying $\int K = 1$ and $\mu_2(K) = \sigma_0^2$ for a fixed positive constant $\sigma_0^2$. The kernel minimizing $R(K) = \int K^2$ subject to these constraints is the **Epanechnikov kernel**

$$
K_E(u) = \frac{3}{4}\,(1 - u^2)\,\mathbf{1}\{|u| \le 1\}, \qquad \mu_2(K_E) = \frac{1}{5}, \quad R(K_E) = \frac{3}{5}.
$$

(By rescaling $h$, the conclusion holds for any fixed $\sigma_0^2 > 0$; the statement with $\sigma_0^2 = 1/5$ fixes the conventional support $[-1, 1]$.)
</TheoremBlock>

<ProofBlock>
Minimizing the quadratic functional $R(K) = \int K(u)^2\,du$ subject to the two linear constraints $\int K = 1$ and $\int u^2 K = \frac{1}{5}$ plus the symmetry and nonnegativity constraints, we form the Lagrangian

$$
\mathcal{L}[K] = \int K(u)^2\,du + \lambda_0\!\left(\int K - 1\right) + \lambda_2\!\left(\int u^2 K - \frac{1}{5}\right).
$$

Symmetry fixes the first-moment multiplier at zero. The pointwise Euler–Lagrange first-order condition is $2 K(u) + \lambda_0 + \lambda_2 u^2 = 0$, giving

$$
K(u) = -\frac{\lambda_0}{2} - \frac{\lambda_2}{2}\, u^2 = a - b\, u^2
$$

on the region where this is positive, with $K = 0$ outside. Write $K(u) = a(1 - u^2/r^2)\,\mathbf{1}\{|u| \le r\}$, where $r := \sqrt{a/b}$ is the support radius (forced by $K(\pm r) = 0$). The two constraints pin $a$ and $r$:

$$
\int K = a \int_{-r}^{r}(1 - u^2/r^2)\,du = a\,\frac{4r}{3} = 1 \implies a = \frac{3}{4r},
$$

$$
\int u^2 K = a \int_{-r}^{r}(u^2 - u^4/r^2)\,du = a\left(\frac{2r^3}{3} - \frac{2r^3}{5}\right) = \frac{4ar^3}{15} = \frac{r^2}{5}\quad\text{(using $a = 3/(4r)$).}
$$

Setting $\mu_2(K) = 1/5$ gives $r^2 = 1$, so $r = 1$ and $a = 3/4$, yielding

$$
K_E(u) = \frac{3}{4}\,(1 - u^2)\, \mathbf{1}\{|u| \le 1\}.
$$

The second-derivative test is automatic: the objective $\int K^2$ is strictly convex in $K$, so the stationary point is a global minimum. Computing the optimum:

$$
R(K_E) = \int_{-1}^{1} \frac{9}{16}(1 - u^2)^2\,du = \frac{9}{16} \cdot \frac{16}{15} = \frac{3}{5}.
$$

$\blacksquare$ — using Lagrangian variational principles with linear constraints and nonnegativity, the Euler–Lagrange condition for a quadratic objective, and elementary Calculus-of-Variations (EPA1969; WAN1995 §2.7).
</ProofBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={10} title="Why the n^(-1/5) rate is slower than parametric">
A parametric MLE of a Normal mean has MSE $O(n^{-1})$ — the square of the standard $\sqrt n$ convergence. The KDE's AMISE rate $O(n^{-4/5})$ is slower because we're estimating an *entire function*, not a finite-dimensional parameter. The rate is a direct consequence of Theorem 4: the bias–variance trade-off forces $h \to 0$ at rate $n^{-1/5}$, not the $n^{-1/2}$ that parametric estimators enjoy. In high dimensions the rate gets worse still — Scott 2015 Ch. 6 derives the multivariate rate $O(n^{-4/(4+d)})$, which we'll pointer-forward in §30.10.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={11} title="Structural parallel to Bahadur linearization">
The AMISE-optimal $h^\ast$ is the first-order condition on a quadratic-in-$h^2$ loss function. The structural parallel to the [Bahadur representation of the sample quantile](/topics/order-statistics-and-quantiles#section-29-6) (Topic 29 §29.6) is that *both* reduce nonparametric inference to a balance between a variance term that shrinks with more data and a bias term controlled by an asymptotic parameter: bandwidth $h$ in the KDE case, remainder-term order in the Bahadur case. Every Track 8 result is a version of this trade-off — bootstrap, empirical processes, and density-ratio estimation each have their own "h": a resample count, a bracketing radius, a regularization strength.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={12} title="Kernel choice negligibility">
Theorem 5 minimizes $R(K)$ subject to $\mu_2(K) = \sigma_0^2$. Substituting into $\mathrm{AMISE}^\ast$, the kernel enters through $\{R(K)^4 \mu_2(K)^2\}^{1/5}$. For the five standard kernels, this combined factor ranges from $0.98^{1/5}$ (uniform) to $1.00$ (Epanechnikov) — a spread of under 2%. In log-space, switching kernels moves AMISE$^\ast$ by less than 0.01. Bandwidth choice, by contrast, can move AMISE by an order of magnitude. The practical advice: pick any standard kernel (Gaussian for simplicity, Epanechnikov for compact support), and spend your energy on bandwidth selection (§30.8).
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={13} title="Higher-order kernels and faster rates">
A **fourth-order kernel** is a kernel with $\mu_2(K) = 0$; the bias in Theorem 3 then vanishes to leading order, and the next term is $O(h^4)$ involving $f^{(4)}$. The AMISE rate improves from $n^{-4/5}$ to $n^{-8/9}$ — a meaningful speedup at large $n$. The catch: a fourth-order kernel must take negative values (to integrate $u^2 K = 0$ with $\int K = 1$), so $\hat f_h$ can be negative. In practice this is usually patched by truncation, but Topic 30 follows the standard monograph convention of restricting to second-order kernels. Serfling 1980 §1.11 and Wand–Jones 1995 §2.8 cover higher-order kernels at length; formalml may return to them in the context of density-ratio estimation.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.7 Pointwise asymptotic normality and confidence intervals

Theorem 4 gives us the MISE rate; Theorem 6 gives us pointwise asymptotic normality, from which Gaussian-calibrated confidence intervals for $f(x)$ follow immediately. The proof technique — Lindeberg–Feller for triangular arrays — is the same machinery that powered Topic 11 §11.6's general CLT; we state it and sketch the verification.

<TheoremBlock number={6} title="Parzen's pointwise asymptotic normality (stated with sketch)">
Under the hypotheses of Theorem 4 (symmetric kernel satisfying $\int K = 1$, $\int u K = 0$, $\mu_2(K) < \infty$, $R(K) < \infty$; $f$ continuous at $x$ with $f(x) > 0$), and under the bandwidth regime $h \to 0$, $nh \to \infty$,

$$
\sqrt{nh}\,\Bigl(\hat f_h(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat f_h(x)]\Bigr) \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; f(x)\, R(K)\right).
$$
</TheoremBlock>

<ProofBlock>
Write $\hat f_h(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Z_{n,i}$, where $Z_{n,i} := h^{-1} K((x - X_i)/h)$ form a **triangular array** of iid random variables — iid within each row indexed by $n$, but their distribution depends on $n$ through the bandwidth $h = h(n)$. The variance per term is

$$
\mathrm{Var}(Z_{n,i}) = \frac{f(x)\,R(K)}{h}\,(1 + o(1)),
$$

from Theorem 3 (Step (ii) of Theorem 4's proof, multiplied through by the $h^{-1}$ normalization). Multiplying the standardizing factor $\sqrt{nh}$ onto the centered sum gives

$$
\sqrt{nh}\,\bigl(\hat f_h(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat f_h(x)]\bigr) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n/h}}\sum_{i=1}^n (Z_{n,i} - \mathbb{E}[Z_{n,i}]),
$$

whose variance is $\frac{nh}{n} \cdot \mathrm{Var}(Z_{n,i}) = h \cdot f(x) R(K)/h \cdot (1 + o(1)) = f(x)\,R(K) + o(1)$. Apply the **Lindeberg–Feller central limit theorem** for triangular arrays (Topic 11 §11.6 Thm 2): check the Lindeberg condition

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty}\; \frac{n}{f(x)\,R(K)}\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\bigl(Z_{n,1}/\sqrt{n/h} - \mathbb{E}[Z_{n,1}]/\sqrt{n/h}\bigr)^2\, \mathbf{1}\{\cdots > \varepsilon\}\right] = 0 \quad \text{for all } \varepsilon > 0.
$$

Boundedness of $K$ (compact support in the Epanechnikov case, or $e^{-u^2/2}$ decay in the Gaussian case) ensures $|Z_{n,i}| \le \|K\|_\infty / h$, and the event inside the indicator becomes empty for $n$ large enough because the standardized summand is $O(h^{-1/2}/\sqrt{n}) = O(1/\sqrt{nh}) \to 0$. The Lindeberg condition holds.

$\blacksquare$ — using the Lindeberg–Feller CLT for triangular arrays (Topic 11 §11.6 Thm 2), the variance calculation from Thm 3, and boundedness of $K$ (PAR1962 §3–§5; vdV2000 §24.1).
</ProofBlock>

Theorem 6 centers the CI at $\mathbb{E}[\hat f_h(x)]$, not at $f(x)$. At finite $n$ there is a bias shift of magnitude $\frac{h^2}{2}\mu_2(K) f''(x)$, and the resulting pointwise CI has coverage slightly below the nominal $1 - \alpha$. For the purposes of this topic, we ignore the bias correction and use the plug-in variance

$$
\hat V(x) \;=\; \frac{\hat f_h(x)\, R(K)}{n\,h}, \qquad \mathrm{CI}_{1-\alpha}(x) \;=\; \hat f_h(x) \;\pm\; z_{1 - \alpha/2}\,\sqrt{\hat V(x)},
$$

noting the coverage will be biased-low at modest $n$ and small $h$.

<ExampleBlock number={8} title="Pointwise CI at x = 0 for N(0,1), n = 500">
For a Normal$(0, 1)$ sample of size $n = 500$ (seed-42 NumPy draw, $\hat\sigma \approx 0.98$), Silverman's bandwidth $\hat h_{\mathrm{ROT}} \approx 0.300$. At $x = 0$:

$$
\hat f_h(0) \approx 0.388, \qquad \hat V(0) = \frac{0.388 \cdot 0.2821}{500 \cdot 0.300} \approx 7.30 \times 10^{-4}, \qquad \sqrt{\hat V(0)} \approx 0.027.
$$

The 95% pointwise CI is $0.388 \pm 1.96 \cdot 0.027 = [0.335, 0.441]$. The true density value $f(0) = \varphi(0) \approx 0.399$ lies comfortably inside. This is the T30.11 test pin.
</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={9} title="Density-at-quantile: Bahadur's plug-in (discharge of Topic 29 §29.6 Rem 12)">
[Topic 29 §29.6 Rem 12](/topics/order-statistics-and-quantiles#section-29-6) named KDE as *the* tool for estimating $f(\xi_p)$ — the density in Bahadur's limit variance $\sigma^2 = p(1 - p)/f(\xi_p)^2$. Here we discharge that promise.

Take a Normal$(0, 1)$ sample of size $n = 500$ and focus on the upper quartile $p = 0.9$. The [Type-7 sample quantile](/topics/order-statistics-and-quantiles#section-29-4) is $\hat\xi_{0.9} \approx 1.30$. The Silverman-bandwidth KDE evaluated at that quantile is

$$
\hat f_{\hat h}(\hat\xi_{0.9}) \approx 0.175,
$$

giving the Bahadur-plug-in asymptotic standard error

$$
\hat{\mathrm{SE}}(\hat\xi_{0.9}) \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{0.9 \cdot 0.1}{500 \cdot 0.175^2}} \;\approx\; 0.0242.
$$

The resulting 95% asymptotic CI is $1.30 \pm 1.96 \cdot 0.0242 = [1.25, 1.35]$. For comparison, the [distribution-free binomial-exact CI](/topics/order-statistics-and-quantiles#section-29-7) from Topic 29 §29.7 is $[X_{(r)}, X_{(s)}]$ with $r$ and $s$ chosen so the coverage is at least $1 - \alpha$; at $n = 500$, $p = 0.9$, $\alpha = 0.05$ the CI is approximately $[1.24, 1.37]$. The Bahadur-plug-in CI is noticeably tighter — asymptotic efficiency at the cost of nominal-coverage validity at finite $n$.
</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={14} title="Why the CI has biased coverage at small n">
The CI is centered at $\hat f_h(x)$, which is a consistent estimator of $\mathbb{E}[\hat f_h(x)] = f(x) + \frac{h^2}{2}\mu_2(K) f''(x) + o(h^2)$ — not $f(x)$ itself. At the oracle bandwidth $h^\ast$ the bias is $O(n^{-2/5})$, and the interval width is $O(n^{-2/5})$ as well, so the bias is of the same order as the width. Coverage improves as $n$ grows (the $o(h^2)$ terms shrink faster than the width) but remains biased-low at the finite $n$ ML practitioners see in practice. Bias-corrected CIs (plug-in estimates of $f''$, or undersmoothing) exist; vdV2000 §24.1 is a standard reference.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={15} title="Simultaneous CIs and the bootstrap">
Theorem 6 is pointwise — it gives a CI for $f(x_0)$ at a single preselected $x_0$. For a joint CI covering $f$ over a range $[a, b]$ at level $1 - \alpha$, we need Bickel–Rosenblatt-type machinery, which turns out to be a functional CLT in the same Donsker-theory framework Topic 29 §29.8 used for the Kolmogorov limit. The cleanest route in practice is the smooth bootstrap (§30.10 Ex 13), which constructs simultaneous envelopes by resampling from $\hat f_h$ itself. The full treatment is forthcoming on formalml.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.8 Data-driven bandwidth selection

The oracle $h^\ast$ from Theorem 4 requires the curvature integral $R(f'')$, which the practitioner does not know. Data-driven bandwidth selectors estimate $R(f'')$ — or skip it entirely — and return a data-dependent $\hat h$. The four standard selectors are:

- **Silverman's rule-of-thumb** (the default in R's `bw.nrd0`): a Normal-scale plug-in that assumes $f \approx N(\mu, \sigma^2)$.
- **Scott's rule**: Silverman without the IQR-robustness adjustment.
- **Unbiased cross-validation (UCV)**: minimizes an estimator of MISE $-$ ∫$f^2$.
- **Sheather–Jones plug-in (SHJ)**: a one-step direct plug-in that estimates $R(f'')$ from a pilot KDE.

Silverman and Scott are cheap closed forms; UCV is a grid search at cost $O(n^2 G)$ per evaluation; SHJ is an $O(n^2)$ double-sum followed by a closed-form plug-in. All four are implemented in `nonparametric.ts` §10 or inlined in `PluginBandwidthComparator` (§5.3).

<DefinitionBlock number={4} title="Mean integrated squared error (MISE)">
For an estimator $\hat f_h$ of a density $f$, the **mean integrated squared error** is

$$
\mathrm{MISE}(h) \;:=\; \mathbb{E}\!\int\bigl(\hat f_h(x) - f(x)\bigr)^2\,dx.
$$

The AMISE from §30.5 is the leading-order approximation to MISE; bandwidth selectors typically minimize one or the other. Note $\mathrm{MISE}(h) = \int \mathrm{Var}(\hat f_h(x))\,dx + \int \mathrm{Bias}^2(x)\,dx$ — the integrated decomposition that drives everything in Theorem 4.
</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={7} title="Silverman's rule of thumb">
Under the Normal-scale reference rule — assume $f$ is Normal$(\mu, \sigma^2)$ — the AMISE-optimal bandwidth (Theorem 4) with Gaussian kernel simplifies to

$$
h^\ast_{\text{Silverman}} \;=\; \left(\frac{4}{3 n}\right)^{\!1/5} \hat\sigma \;\approx\; 1.06\, \hat\sigma\, n^{-1/5},
$$

where $\hat\sigma$ is a scale estimate. **Silverman's robust variant** uses $\hat\sigma = \min(\mathrm{SD}, \mathrm{IQR}/1.34)$ to guard against heavy tails or mild bimodality. *Stated*; the derivation substitutes $R(\varphi'') = 3/(8\sqrt\pi)$ into Theorem 4 and rearranges. (SIL1986 §3.4.2)
</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={10} title="Silverman over-smooths bimodal mixtures">
Take a 50/50 mixture of Normal$(-1.5, 1)$ and Normal$(1.5, 1)$, $n = 200$. This is the `bimodalNormalPreset` from `nonparametric-data.ts` — the overall standard deviation is $\hat\sigma \approx \sqrt{1 + 1.5^2} \approx 1.80$, so Silverman gives

$$
\hat h_{\mathrm{Silverman}} \;=\; 1.06 \cdot 1.80 \cdot 200^{-1/5} \;\approx\; 0.66.
$$

But the oracle bandwidth $h^\ast$ (Theorem 4, using the closed-form $R(f'') \approx 0.092$ for this mixture) is $\approx 0.38$. Silverman is nearly $2\times$ too large; the resulting KDE merges the two modes into a single plateau. UCV and Sheather–Jones both estimate $R(f'')$ from the data rather than assuming a Normal reference, and both recover the bimodality cleanly. Figure 8 shows the three side by side.
</ExampleBlock>

Sheather-Jones (violet) recovers both modes cleanly. True density overlaid in black.](/images/topics/kernel-density-estimation/30-bandwidth-selectors-bimodal.png)

<RemarkBlock number={16} title="Why Silverman over-smooths bimodals">
Silverman's rule substitutes a Normal reference for $R(f'')$: for $f = N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, $R(f'') = 3/(8\sqrt\pi \sigma^5)$. For a bimodal density with separation $|\mu_1 - \mu_2| \approx 3\sigma$, the true $R(f'')$ can be $5$–$10\times$ larger than the Normal-reference value — the two modes create sharp peaks and sharp valleys that contribute heavily to $\int f''^2$. Silverman therefore underestimates $R(f'')$ by the same factor, which puts $\hat h$ roughly $(5\text{–}10)^{1/5} \approx 1.4$–$1.6\times$ too large. The fix is to estimate $R(f'')$ from the data — UCV does this implicitly (by cross-validating against the sample), SHJ does it explicitly (via a pilot KDE's second derivative). The `PluginBandwidthComparator` lets you see the effect directly: switch presets and watch the Silverman bandwidth diverge from the oracle on the bimodal case, match it on the unimodal case.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.9 ML bridges: anomaly detection and density-based classification

KDE is a direct ingredient in two classic ML workflows — unsupervised anomaly detection and the plug-in Bayes classifier — and an indirect one in modern neural density estimators. We describe both direct cases concretely; the connection to Nadaraya–Watson regression and mean-shift clustering is a forward pointer.

<ExampleBlock number={11} title="Anomaly detection via low-density flagging">
Given a training sample $X_1, \dots, X_n$ representing "normal" observations, fit a Gaussian KDE $\hat f_h$ with Silverman bandwidth. At test time, flag a new point $x$ as anomalous if $\hat f_h(x) < t$ for some threshold $t$. A natural choice is $t$ = the empirical 5%-quantile of $\{\hat f_h(X_i) : i = 1, \dots, n\}$ — the KDE density values *at the training points themselves*. Under this choice, approximately 5% of training points are (ex post) flagged, and new points that fall into regions of similar sparsity are flagged at test time.

This is the kernel-density-estimate version of **one-class classification**; it requires no labeled anomalies, only a clean sample of normals. In production, practitioners usually replace KDE with a denser estimator (Gaussian Mixture Models, Isolation Forest, Autoencoders) as $n$ grows into the millions, but the KDE version remains the pedagogical entry point and a useful sanity check at low $n$.
</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={12} title="Density-based Bayes classifier and naïve Bayes">
The **plug-in Bayes classifier** (Fix–Hodges 1951) estimates class-conditional densities $\hat f_{h, c}(x)$ via a separate KDE on each class's training sample, then assigns test point $x$ to

$$
\hat c(x) \;=\; \arg\max_c\; \hat\pi_c \cdot \hat f_{h, c}(x),
$$

where $\hat\pi_c = n_c/n$ is the estimated class prior. In $d$ dimensions, full multivariate KDE suffers from the curse of dimensionality (§30.10 Rem 21), so **naïve Bayes with KDE** factors the class density as $\hat f_{h, c}(x) = \prod_{j=1}^d \hat f_{h, c, j}(x_j)$ — a product of $d$ univariate KDEs, one per feature. Naïve-Bayes-with-KDE is the standard ML baseline for mixed continuous-discrete features; `scikit-learn` ships it as `KernelDensity` + `ClassifierMixin`.

Compared to parametric discriminant analysis (LDA, QDA from Topic 15), the KDE version trades parametric efficiency for distribution-freeness — it works on class densities that are skewed, bimodal, or otherwise non-Normal, without any parametric assumption.
</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={17} title="Kernel regression via Nadaraya–Watson">
The covariate-conditional extension of KDE is **kernel regression** (Nadaraya 1964; Watson 1964):

$$
\hat m_h(x) \;=\; \frac{\sum_i Y_i\, K_h(x - X_i)}{\sum_i K_h(x - X_i)}.
$$

This is a weighted average of the $Y_i$ values, with weights decaying as $|x - X_i|$ grows. It is the kernel-smoothing companion to the KDE: KDE estimates the marginal density $f(x)$, NW estimates the conditional expectation $E[Y \mid X = x]$. The two share the Theorem 3 bias–variance structure and the Theorem 6 asymptotic-normality story, so everything we built for $\hat f_h$ carries over. The full development — including local polynomial refinements — is the forthcoming formalml *kernel-regression* chapter.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={18} title="Mean-shift clustering">
**Mean-shift** (Fukunaga–Hostetler 1975; Comaniciu–Meer 2002) is an unsupervised clustering algorithm that performs gradient ascent on $\hat f_h$. Starting from each data point, iterate $x \leftarrow x + h \cdot \nabla \log \hat f_h(x)$ until convergence; the fixed points are the local modes of the KDE, and points that converge to the same mode are assigned to the same cluster. The advantage over k-means is that mean-shift does not require specifying $k$ in advance — the number of modes *is* the number of clusters. The disadvantage is computational: gradient ascent + KDE evaluation is $O(n^2)$ per iteration, so it doesn't scale past modest $n$. Modern variants (spectral mean-shift, blurring mean-shift) are forthcoming on formalml.
</RemarkBlock>

## 30.10 Track 8 forward-map and the KDE → bootstrap bridge

Topic 30 closes with a forward-pointer figure (Fig 9) and a set of remarks that place KDE inside the larger Track 8 spine. Topic 29 (order statistics) opened the track; Topic 30 (KDE) is the mid-track hinge; Topics 31 (bootstrap) and 32 (empirical processes) close it. Beyond Track 8 itself, KDE is a load-bearing ingredient in several formalml chapters — kernel regression, local regression, normalizing flows, clustering, density-ratio estimation — each of which extends the KDE framework to a different ML-relevant context.

<ExampleBlock number={13} title="Smooth bootstrap: resampling from the KDE">
The standard nonparametric bootstrap resamples from the empirical distribution $F_n$ — a step function that places mass $1/n$ at each $X_i$. Silverman–Young 1987's **smooth bootstrap** resamples from $\hat f_h$ instead: draw $X_i^\ast \sim \hat f_h$ by first picking an observation $X_{J_i}$ uniformly from the sample (where $J_i$ is uniform on $\{1, \dots, n\}$) and then perturbing it by an independent draw from the kernel $K_h$:

$$
X_i^\ast \;=\; X_{J_i} \;+\; h \cdot U_i, \qquad U_i \sim K, \text{ iid}.
$$

The resulting bootstrap distribution is smoothly supported rather than concentrated on $\{X_1, \dots, X_n\}$, which matters when the statistic of interest depends on derivatives or local continuity — e.g., quantile regression estimators or mode estimators. Topic 31 develops the full bootstrap framework; smooth-bootstrap is one of several refinements it covers. The KDE → bootstrap bridge is exactly the Topic 30 → 31 edge in the Track 8 forward-map.
</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={19} title="Topic 31: Bootstrap">
The bootstrap (Efron 1979) is the Track 8 closer for parametric-inference questions. It resamples from the empirical distribution (or, in the smooth variant, from $\hat f_h$) to simulate the sampling distribution of a statistic without invoking asymptotic normality. Topic 31 will develop nonparametric and parametric bootstraps, bootstrap CIs (percentile, BCa, studentized), and the Edgeworth-expansion-based analysis of bootstrap accuracy. Expect Topic 30's bandwidth selectors to reappear as pilot estimators for smooth bootstraps of sample-quantile statistics.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={20} title="Normalizing flows: learned density estimators">
Modern neural density estimators — normalizing flows (Rezende–Mohamed 2015; Papamakarios et al. 2021) — replace the kernel-average-of-bumps $\hat f_h$ with a learned invertible transformation $g_\theta$ from a simple base density to the target: $\hat f(x) = p_{\text{base}}(g_\theta^{-1}(x)) |\det \partial g_\theta^{-1}/\partial x|$. The AMISE framework of §30.6 doesn't directly apply — flows are parametric within a specified flow family — but the bias-vs-capacity intuition carries over. The forthcoming formalml *normalizing-flows* chapter will contrast the two estimator classes head-to-head; they have complementary strengths (KDE at low $n$ and low $d$; flows at high $n$ and moderate $d$).
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={21} title="Multivariate KDE and the curse of dimensionality">
In $d$ dimensions, the KDE generalizes via a product kernel or a full bandwidth matrix $\mathbf{H}$: $\hat f_\mathbf{H}(\mathbf{x}) = n^{-1} |\mathbf{H}|^{-1} \sum_i K(\mathbf{H}^{-1}(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{X}_i))$. The AMISE rate deteriorates to $O(n^{-4/(4+d)})$ — the **curse of dimensionality** for density estimation. At $d = 10$, $n = 1000$ is effectively $n^{4/14} \approx 6$ in the univariate-equivalent rate, which is why high-dimensional density estimation is hard. Duong 2007's `ks` R package is the standard multivariate implementation; Scott 2015 Ch. 6 is the standard reference. The forthcoming formalml *density-estimation-multivariate* chapter treats the full story.
</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={22} title="Density-ratio estimation and covariate shift">
In many ML problems we care about the ratio $r(x) = p(x)/q(x)$ rather than the densities themselves — e.g., importance weighting for covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, or generative-adversarial-network discriminator outputs. The naïve plug-in $\hat r(x) = \hat f^{(p)}_h(x)/\hat f^{(q)}_h(x)$ has the same $n^{-4/5}$ rate but amplifies bias in the tails. Direct density-ratio estimation (Sugiyama–Suzuki–Kanamori 2012) sidesteps the denominator entirely by fitting $r$ directly via loss minimization. The full treatment is forthcoming on formalml as *density-ratio-estimation*; Topic 30's KDE machinery is the starting point.
</RemarkBlock>
