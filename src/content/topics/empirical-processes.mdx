## 32.1 From pointwise to uniform: motivation

{/* SCAFFOLD §32.1
  - Opening: empirical-risk-minimisation (ERM) as the motivating ML problem.
  - Contrast pointwise LLN (one $\theta$) vs uniform LLN (sup over $\theta$).
  - Fig 1 (32-pointwise-vs-uniform) sits here.
  - Ex 1: ERM — why uniform convergence is the right consistency statement.
  - Ex 2: goodness-of-fit — sup-distance KS statistic.
  - Rem 1: ML anchor — generalization-bound teaser to formalML.
  - Rem 2: Track-8 bridge — how this chapter relates to Topics 29–31.
*/}

Every machine-learning paper that trains a model by empirical risk minimisation (ERM) — which is to say, most of them — is quietly asking an empirical-process question. Fix a loss $\ell$, a parameter space $\Theta$, and iid data $X_1, \dots, X_n$; the population risk $R(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[\ell(X; \theta)]$ is what we want to minimise, and the empirical risk $\hat R_n(\theta) = n^{-1} \sum_i \ell(X_i; \theta)$ is what we can compute. ERM picks the minimiser of $\hat R_n$ and hopes that choice tracks the minimiser of $R$. Whether it does depends on whether $\hat R_n \to R$ just *pointwise* (for each fixed $\theta$, the ordinary LLN suffices) or *uniformly* over $\Theta$ (the sup-gap shrinks). The distinction is not cosmetic: pointwise convergence alone can coexist with $\hat R_n$'s minimiser drifting arbitrarily far from $R$'s, and Topic 32 exists to work out when the uniform version holds — and what happens when we take the difference $\hat R_n - R$ seriously as a random function, not just a random number.

![Two-panel contrast. Left: single trajectory of $\hat R_n(\theta)$ at a fixed $\theta$, converging to $R(\theta)$ by the LLN. Right: five trajectories at five different $\theta$ values; the sup-gap $\sup_\theta |\hat R_n - R|$ does not shrink nearly as fast as any single trajectory.](/images/topics/empirical-processes/32-pointwise-vs-uniform.png)

*Figure 1. Pointwise vs uniform convergence. Left: a single $\theta$; the sample-risk trajectory settles to the true risk by the LLN. Right: five $\theta$ values simultaneously; at every $n$ the **sup**-gap over the five is larger than any individual trajectory's deviation, and the sup-gap decays more slowly. ERM selects whichever $\theta$ minimises $\hat R_n$, and without uniform convergence there is no guarantee that minimiser tracks the true-risk minimiser.*

<ExampleBlock number={1} title="ERM and the need for uniform convergence">

Take $X \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$ and squared loss $\ell(x; \theta) = (x - \theta)^2$, so $R(\theta) = \mathrm{Var}(X) + (\mathbb{E} X - \theta)^2$ is minimised at $\theta^\star = 1/2$. The LLN gives $\hat R_n(\theta) \to R(\theta)$ a.s. for every fixed $\theta$. But the minimiser $\hat\theta_n$ of $\hat R_n$ is just the sample mean $\bar X_n$, which by the CLT sits within $O(n^{-1/2})$ of $\theta^\star$ — the *same* $\sqrt n$-rate that controls $\sup_\theta |\hat R_n(\theta) - R(\theta)|$. This isn't a coincidence: the rate at which $\hat\theta_n \to \theta^\star$ is controlled by the uniform gap, not the pointwise one. Pointwise LLN on its own gives consistency-in-parameter; it does *not* give a rate on that consistency. §32.4 supplies the precise condition — finite VC dimension of the loss class — that makes the uniform-convergence rate quantitative.

</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={2} title="Goodness-of-fit as a sup-statistic">

Topic 29's Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic $D_n = \sup_t |F_n(t) - F(t)|$ is the empirical-process object in disguise. Under the null $X_i \sim F$, Glivenko–Cantelli (Topic 10 §10.7) gives $D_n \to 0$ a.s. — the uniform LLN for the distribution-function class $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}} = \{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$. Donsker's theorem (§32.5) supplies the corresponding uniform CLT: $\sqrt n\,D_n$ converges in distribution to the sup of a Brownian bridge, the Kolmogorov distribution Topic 29 §29.8 used to build KS's distribution-free critical values. The step from "fix a $t$, apply the LLN or CLT" to "take a sup over all $t$, get a uniform analogue" is the leap Topic 32 formalises in generality, for *any* function class $\mathcal{F}$ not just $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={1} title="ML anchor — uniform convergence underwrites generalization">

Generalisation in machine learning is the same question ERM asks, wearing a different hat: does the training loss on an IID training set track the test loss *uniformly* over the hypothesis class we're searching? When the class has finite VC dimension $V$, §32.4 Thm 2 delivers a positive answer with an explicit $O(\sqrt{V \log n / n})$ rate; when it doesn't — think of neural networks with more parameters than data points — Rademacher-complexity refinements and PAC-Bayes bounds take over, and both reuse §32.4's symmetrisation technique nearly verbatim. Those refinements live at formalML's [generalization-bounds](https://formalml.com/topics/generalization-bounds) and [pac-bayes-bounds](https://formalml.com/topics/pac-bayes-bounds); §32.4 is the version of the result this site proves in full.

</RemarkBlock>

## 32.2 The empirical process $\mathbb{G}_n$ and function classes

{/* SCAFFOLD §32.2
  - Opening: generalize from $F_n(t)$ to $P_n f$; introduce the empirical measure $P_n$.
  - Def 1: empirical measure $P_n := n^{-1} \sum \delta_{X_i}$.
  - Def 2: empirical process $\mathbb{G}_n := \sqrt n (P_n - P)$; evaluated at a class $\mathcal{F}$.
  - Thm (stated, 1) — pointwise CLT for $\mathbb{G}_n(f)$ when $Pf^2 < \infty$. Applies $\sqrt n (P_n f - P f) \to \mathcal{N}(0, \mathrm{Var}_P f)$.
  - Fig 2 (32-gc-1d-warmup): 1D Glivenko–Cantelli preview at $n \in \{20, 100, 500, 2000\}$.
  - Ex 3: distribution-function class $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}} = \{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$.
  - Ex 4: parametric-family indicators $\{\mathbf{1}\{X \le \theta(\alpha)\} : \alpha \in A\}$.
  - Rem 3: from LLN/CLT to uniform versions — the central move of the chapter.
  - Rem 4: why $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ is the ambient space (next section).
*/}

The right way to generalise "the ECDF converges to the CDF" is to stop thinking about a single index point $t \in \mathbb{R}$ and start thinking about an entire *class of functions* $\mathcal{F}$. Topic 29's $F_n(t) = n^{-1}\sum_i \mathbf{1}\{X_i \le t\}$ is the empirical average of a single indicator function evaluated at one $t$; tilt your head and that's the same as $P_n f$ where $P_n$ is the empirical measure and $f = \mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]}$ is one function. Let $f$ range over a class $\mathcal{F}$ and $P_n f$ becomes a whole random function indexed by $\mathcal{F}$, not a random number. Its centred-and-rescaled version is the **empirical process** $\mathbb{G}_n(f) := \sqrt n(P_n f - P f)$, and the central question of Topic 32 is: what can we say about the stochastic-process-on-$\mathcal{F}$ behaviour of $\mathbb{G}_n$ as $n \to \infty$?

<DefinitionBlock number={1} title="Empirical measure $P_n$">

Given iid $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim P$ on a measurable space $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A})$, the **empirical measure** is $P_n := n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^n \delta_{X_i}$, the random probability measure that places mass $1/n$ at each sample point. For any measurable $f : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$, define

$$
P_n f := \int f\,dP_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n f(X_i), \qquad P f := \int f\,dP = \mathbb{E}[f(X)].
$$

Topic 29's ECDF $F_n(t) = P_n(\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]})$ is $P_n$ applied to a single half-line indicator; the sample mean $\bar X_n = P_n(\mathrm{id})$ is $P_n$ applied to the identity; Topic 31's bootstrap draws sample points from $P_n$ rather than from $P$. The empirical measure is the glue holding Track 8 together.

</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={2} title="Empirical process $\mathbb{G}_n$ indexed by $\mathcal{F}$">

Given a class $\mathcal{F}$ of measurable functions $\mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$, the **empirical process** indexed by $\mathcal{F}$ is the random function

$$
\mathbb{G}_n : \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad \mathbb{G}_n f := \sqrt n\,(P_n f - P f) = \frac{1}{\sqrt n}\sum_{i=1}^n \bigl(f(X_i) - P f\bigr).
$$

Two specialisations recover familiar objects. If $\mathcal{F} = \{f\}$ is a singleton, $\mathbb{G}_n f$ is just $\sqrt n$ times the sample-mean deviation of $f(X)$ and the ordinary CLT applies. If $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}} = \{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$, then $\mathbb{G}_n(\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]}) = \sqrt n(F_n(t) - F(t))$ — the $\sqrt n$-rescaled CDF-deviation Topic 29 §29.8 worked with. The empirical process is the uniform-index generalisation of both.

</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={1} title="Pointwise CLT for $\mathbb{G}_n(f)$ (stated)">

For any fixed $f \in \mathcal{F}$ with $P f^2 < \infty$,

$$
\mathbb{G}_n f \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\,\mathrm{Var}_P f\bigr) \qquad \text{as } n \to \infty,
$$

by the ordinary CLT (Topic 11 §11.5 Thm 3). This tells us $\mathbb{G}_n$ evaluated at any *one* $f$ is asymptotically Gaussian; it tells us *nothing* about joint or uniform behaviour across $\mathcal{F}$. Topic 32's two organising theorems — uniform LLN (§32.4) and uniform CLT (§32.5) — fill that gap.

</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={3} title="The distribution-function class $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$">

The distribution-function class $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}} := \{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ is parametrised by the real line, one function per threshold $t$. The empirical process indexed by this class is $\mathbb{G}_n(\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]}) = \sqrt n(F_n(t) - F(t))$, which we'll just write $\mathbb{G}_n(t)$ when no confusion arises. This is the class Donsker's theorem (§32.5 Thm 3) handles in full, discharging the §29.8 IOU. $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ has VC dimension $1$ (see §32.4 Def 6), so it's also the easiest case of §32.4's G-C-for-VC theorem.

</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={4} title="Parametric-family indicators">

Fix a parametric family $\{F_\alpha : \alpha \in A\}$ with $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ and an observable $q \in (0, 1)$. The class $\mathcal{F} = \{\mathbf{1}\{x \le F_\alpha^{-1}(q)\} : \alpha \in A\}$ contains one indicator per candidate $\alpha$, and $P_n f_\alpha = $ ECDF evaluated at the $q$-th quantile under $\alpha$. $\mathcal{F}$ has VC dimension 1 when $\alpha \mapsto F_\alpha^{-1}(q)$ is monotone (which is the normal case — bigger $\alpha$ shifts the quantile one way). §32.4 Thm 2 applies: $\sup_{\alpha \in A} |P_n f_\alpha - P f_\alpha| \to 0$ a.s., a uniform LLN over the whole family. This is the workhorse result that makes MLE consistent over a parametric family — the statistician's specialisation of the ML learning-theory bound.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={3} title="From LLN / CLT to uniform versions">

Pointwise LLN (for each $f$, $P_n f \to P f$ a.s.) does NOT imply uniform LLN ($\sup_f |P_n f - P f| \to 0$ a.s.) — the classical Vapnik–Chervonenkis counterexample takes $\mathcal{X} = [0, 1]$, $P$ = Lebesgue, and $\mathcal{F} = \{\mathbf{1}_A : A \text{ finite}\}$; every $f \in \mathcal{F}$ satisfies $P f = 0$, but $\sup_f |P_n f| = 1$ almost surely (pick $A = \{X_1, \dots, X_n\}$). The class is too rich: you can always find some $f$ that indicator-fits the sample perfectly. §32.4 makes precise the complexity condition — finite VC dimension — that excludes these pathological classes and rescues uniform LLN.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={4} title="Why $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ is the ambient space">

For each $\omega$, the path $f \mapsto \mathbb{G}_n(\omega)(f)$ is a bounded function on $\mathcal{F}$ as long as $\mathcal{F}$ has a finite envelope. It lives naturally in the Banach space $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ of bounded functions $\mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}$ with the sup-norm $\|z\|_\mathcal{F} := \sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} |z(f)|$. The right limit-theorem statement for $\mathbb{G}_n$ is "weak convergence in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$" — a more delicate notion than ordinary weak convergence because the sup itself need not be measurable. §32.3 handles that machinery.

</RemarkBlock>

## 32.3 Weak convergence in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$

{/* SCAFFOLD §32.3 — the notation-density-peak section.
  - Opening: what it means to have a random element of a function space converge weakly.
  - Def 3: $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ = Banach space of bounded $\mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}$ functions with sup-norm.
  - Def 4: outer probability (measurability housekeeping when $\sup_f \mathbb{G}_n f$ isn't measurable).
  - Def 5: weak convergence of a tight random element in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$.
  - Ex 5: non-measurability of $\sup_f \mathbb{G}_n f$ for uncountable $\mathcal{F}$.
  - Rem 5: Hoffmann–Jørgensen outer-probability fix (cite VDW1996 §1.3).
  - Rem 6: why we can ignore the measurability housekeeping in practice (weak convergence holds anyway, statements transfer).
  - **The §32.3 notation table is rendered VERBATIM below — the single most notation-dense section of the curriculum.**
*/}

Topic 9 defined weak convergence for real-valued random variables: $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ means $\mathbb{E} h(X_n) \to \mathbb{E} h(X)$ for every bounded continuous $h$. Upgrading that to random elements of a function space — which is what $\mathbb{G}_n \in \ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ requires — runs into a technical snag: $\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \mathbb{G}_n(f)$ is not, in general, a measurable function on the sample space. Hoffmann–Jørgensen's solution (1984, formalised in vdV–Wellner §1.5) is to work with outer expectation $\mathbb{E}^*$ and to demand that the limit process $\mathbb{G}$ be *tight*. The payoff is a weak-convergence notion strong enough to deliver continuous-mapping theorems and delta-method lifts, which is all §32.4–§32.8 need. The notation table at the end of this section is the chart worth bookmarking before reading on.

<DefinitionBlock number={3} title="The space $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$">

For a set $\mathcal{F}$, the space $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ is the set of bounded functions $z : \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}$, equipped with the sup-norm

$$
\|z\|_\mathcal{F} := \sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} |z(f)|.
$$

$(\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F}), \|\cdot\|_\mathcal{F})$ is a Banach space. The empirical process $\mathbb{G}_n$ is an $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$-valued random element whenever $\mathcal{F}$ has a square-integrable envelope: $\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} |f| \le F$ with $P F^2 < \infty$ (see §32.6 Def 11 for the formal envelope condition).

</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={4} title="Outer probability $\mathbb{P}^*$">

For any subset $A \subseteq \Omega$ of the sample space (measurable or not), define

$$
\mathbb{P}^*(A) := \inf\{\mathbb{P}(B) : B \supseteq A, B \text{ measurable}\}.
$$

For a function $T : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$, the outer expectation is $\mathbb{E}^* T := \inf\{\mathbb{E} S : S \ge T, S \text{ measurable}\}$. When $T$ is measurable, $\mathbb{P}^*$ and $\mathbb{E}^*$ collapse to the ordinary $\mathbb{P}$ and $\mathbb{E}$. The construction is due to Hoffmann–Jørgensen (1984) and handles exactly the case of $\sup_f \mathbb{G}_n(f)$ where the supremum over an uncountable class need not be measurable.

</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={5} title="Weak convergence in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$">

A sequence of $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$-valued random elements $\mathbb{G}_n$ **converges weakly** to a tight random element $\mathbb{G}$, written $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{G}$, if

$$
\mathbb{E}^* h(\mathbb{G}_n) \;\to\; \mathbb{E}\, h(\mathbb{G}) \qquad \text{for every bounded Lipschitz } h : \ell^\infty(\mathcal{F}) \to \mathbb{R}.
$$

"Tight" means $\mathbb{G}$ concentrates its mass on compact subsets: for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there is a compact $K_\varepsilon \subseteq \ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ with $\Pr(\mathbb{G} \notin K_\varepsilon) < \varepsilon$. Tightness is essential because $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ is not separable for uncountable $\mathcal{F}$ — without it the limit law is ill-defined.

</DefinitionBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={5} title="Non-measurability of the sup">

Take $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$, sample $X_1 \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$, and consider the map $\omega \mapsto \sup_{t \in \mathbb{R}} \mathbb{G}_1(\omega)(t) = \sup_t \sqrt 1\,(\mathbf{1}\{X_1 \le t\} - t)$. Since $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ is indexed by uncountably many $t$, the supremum is over an uncountable family of measurable functions and need not itself be measurable in a general measure-theoretic sense (any specific construction using the axiom of choice can produce a non-measurable sup). The fix: work with the *outer* probability $\mathbb{P}^*\bigl(\sup_t \mathbb{G}_n(t) > x\bigr)$ instead. For the classes we actually encounter — VC classes in §32.4, bracketing-finite classes in §32.6 — the supremum *is* measurable, because continuity of the sample paths a.s. allows reduction to a countable dense index set; outer probability collapses back to probability, and no harm is done.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={5} title="Hoffmann–Jørgensen outer-probability fix">

The outer-probability machinery (VDW1996 §1.3, KOS2008 §7) is the standard fix for non-measurable suprema. It's the analog of using the outer Lebesgue measure in real analysis: you give up working with ordinary $\sigma$-additivity on all subsets, but you gain the ability to make probabilistic statements about quantities that aren't technically measurable. Every theorem from §32.4 onwards is stated using $\mathbb{E}^*$ implicitly; we'll suppress the star except where it matters.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={6} title="Why the measurability housekeeping doesn't bite in practice">

For every Donsker class $\mathcal{F}$ we meet in §§32.4–32.8, the limit process $\mathbb{G}_P$ has a.s. uniformly continuous sample paths with respect to the $L^2(P)$ pseudo-metric on $\mathcal{F}$. Continuity lets you recover $\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \mathbb{G}_P(f)$ from the sup over any countable dense subset, and *that* sup is measurable. So all our $\mathbb{P}^*$ statements coincide with ordinary $\mathbb{P}$ statements on the event "the limit process is continuous" — which is a full-probability event. The measurability housekeeping is real, but its contribution to the bottom line is zero.

</RemarkBlock>

The question §32.2 Rem 3 left hanging: *which* function classes admit a uniform LLN? Answer — classes with finite **VC dimension**, a combinatorial complexity measure due to Vapnik and Chervonenkis (1971) that captures how many $\{0, 1\}^n$-labellings of an $n$-point set the class can realise. Rich classes realise all $2^n$ labellings at every $n$ and fail uniform LLN; VC-finite classes realise only polynomially many, and the exponential tail of Hoeffding's inequality (Topic 12) wins against that polynomial with room to spare. The resulting theorem — Glivenko–Cantelli for VC classes — goes by a less formal name in the ML literature: the *fundamental theorem of statistical learning*. See Rem 9.

<DefinitionBlock number={6} title="VC dimension">

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a class of $\{0, 1\}$-valued functions on $\mathcal{X}$. For any finite set $\{x_1, \dots, x_n\} \subseteq \mathcal{X}$, the **restriction** of $\mathcal{F}$ to this set is the finite set of labellings $\{(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n)) : f \in \mathcal{F}\} \subseteq \{0, 1\}^n$. The **shatter coefficient** is

$$
s_{\mathcal{F}}(n) \;:=\; \sup_{x_1, \dots, x_n \in \mathcal{X}} \bigl|\{(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n)) : f \in \mathcal{F}\}\bigr|.
$$

A set $\{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ is **shattered** by $\mathcal{F}$ if its restriction has cardinality $2^n$ — every $\{0,1\}^n$-labelling is realised. The **VC dimension** of $\mathcal{F}$ is the largest $n$ such that some $n$-point set is shattered:

$$
\mathrm{VC}(\mathcal{F}) \;:=\; \sup\{n : s_{\mathcal{F}}(n) = 2^n\}.
$$

If $s_{\mathcal{F}}(n) = 2^n$ for every $n$, the class has infinite VC dimension (and is too rich to be Glivenko–Cantelli).

</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={7} title="Glivenko–Cantelli class">

A class $\mathcal{F}$ is a **Glivenko–Cantelli class for $P$** (or *$P$-GC*) if

$$
\|P_n - P\|_\mathcal{F} \;=\; \sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} |P_n f - P f| \;\xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}\; 0 \qquad \text{as } n \to \infty.
$$

Equivalently: the uniform LLN holds on $\mathcal{F}$ under $P$. Thm 2 below says that any VC-finite class is $P$-GC *for every* $P$ — uniformity is both over $f \in \mathcal{F}$ *and* over the distribution. That distribution-free property is what makes the result useful in ML, where we never know $P$.

</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={1} title="Sauer–Shelah–Vapnik (stated)">

Let $\mathcal{F}$ have VC dimension $V < \infty$. Then for every $n \ge 1$,

$$
s_{\mathcal{F}}(n) \;\le\; \sum_{k=0}^{V} \binom{n}{k} \;\le\; (n + 1)^V.
$$

The shatter coefficient — which *a priori* could grow as fast as $2^n$ — is forced into polynomial growth in $n$ the moment the VC dimension is finite. This is the pigeonhole miracle that makes §32.4 Thm 2 work: the finite-class bound in Step 4 of the proof below only costs a factor of $s_{\mathcal{F}}(n) \le (n+1)^V$, not $2^n$.

**Proof (sketch).** The classical argument is by induction on the size of the sample set, with the inductive step carefully tracking which subsets remain shattered under a one-point extension. Full proof in SAU1972 (3 pages, self-contained); independently in SHE1972 and VAP1971. We omit it here — the proof is elegant but the combinatorics don't carry over to Donsker or the delta method, so it doesn't earn its real estate in a topic whose mathematical through-line is fidi+tightness.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={2} title="Glivenko–Cantelli for VC classes (Vapnik–Chervonenkis 1971)">

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a class of $\{0, 1\}$-valued measurable functions on $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A})$ with finite VC dimension $V := \mathrm{VC}(\mathcal{F})$. Then, for any probability measure $P$ on $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A})$,

$$
\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} |P_n f - P f| \;\xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}\; 0 \qquad \text{as } n \to \infty.
$$

</TheoremBlock>

<ProofBlock number={1}>

The argument is a five-step chain: **symmetrize** (replace $P$ by an independent ghost sample), **randomize** (multiply by Rademacher signs), **reduce to a finite class** (Sauer–Shelah–Vapnik), **bound the finite class** (Hoeffding + union bound), and **integrate the exponential tail** (Borel–Cantelli).

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and let $\|\cdot\|_{\mathcal{F}}$ denote the sup-norm $\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} |\cdot|$ on $\mathcal{F}$.

**Step 1 — symmetrization.** Introduce an independent ghost sample $X_1', \dots, X_n'$ with the same distribution $P$, and let $P_n' := n^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^n \delta_{X_i'}$. For any $\varepsilon > 0$ with $n \varepsilon^2 \geq 2$, the symmetrization lemma (vdV–Wellner Lem 2.3.7) gives

$$
\Pr\bigl(\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} > \varepsilon\bigr) \;\leq\; 2\,\Pr\bigl(\|P_n - P_n'\|_{\mathcal{F}} > \varepsilon / 2\bigr).
$$

**Step 2 — Rademacher randomization.** Let $\xi_1, \dots, \xi_n$ be iid Rademacher ($\pm 1$ each with probability $1/2$), independent of all $X_i, X_i'$. By exchangeability of $(X_i, X_i')$ pairs under the sign flip:

$$
\Pr\bigl(\|P_n - P_n'\|_{\mathcal{F}} > \varepsilon / 2\bigr) \;\leq\; 2\,\Pr\Bigl(\Bigl\|\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \xi_i f(X_i)\Bigr\|_{\mathcal{F}} > \varepsilon / 4\Bigr).
$$

Call the random function $f \mapsto n^{-1} \sum_i \xi_i f(X_i)$ the **Rademacher process** $P_n^\xi$.

**Step 3 — reduce to a finite class.** Condition on $X_1, \dots, X_n$. The restriction of $\mathcal{F}$ to $\{X_1, \dots, X_n\}$ — the set of distinct $\{0, 1\}^n$-valued vectors $(f(X_1), \dots, f(X_n))$ as $f$ ranges over $\mathcal{F}$ — has cardinality at most the shatter coefficient $s_{\mathcal{F}}(n)$. By Sauer–Shelah–Vapnik (Thm 1),

$$
s_{\mathcal{F}}(n) \;\leq\; \sum_{k=0}^{V} \binom{n}{k} \;\leq\; (n + 1)^V \qquad \text{for all } n \geq V.
$$

**Step 4 — bound the finite class.** For each fixed vector $(f(X_1), \dots, f(X_n)) \in \{0, 1\}^n$, $P_n^\xi f = n^{-1} \sum_i \xi_i f(X_i)$ is a bounded average of iid mean-zero $\pm 1$-valued random variables — specifically, each summand $\xi_i f(X_i) \in [-1, 1]$. Hoeffding's inequality (Topic 12 §12.3) gives, conditionally on $X_1, \dots, X_n$,

$$
\Pr_\xi\bigl(|P_n^\xi f| > \varepsilon / 4 \,\big|\, X_{1:n}\bigr) \;\leq\; 2\,\exp\!\bigl(-n\,\varepsilon^2 / 32\bigr).
$$

Union-bounding over the at-most-$s_{\mathcal{F}}(n)$ distinct $(f(X_i))_i$ vectors,

$$
\Pr_\xi\bigl(\|P_n^\xi\|_{\mathcal{F}} > \varepsilon / 4 \,\big|\, X_{1:n}\bigr) \;\leq\; 2\,s_{\mathcal{F}}(n)\,\exp\!\bigl(-n\,\varepsilon^2 / 32\bigr) \;\leq\; 2(n + 1)^V\,\exp\!\bigl(-n\,\varepsilon^2 / 32\bigr).
$$

Taking expectation over $X_{1:n}$ preserves the bound.

**Step 5 — combine and apply Borel–Cantelli.** Chaining the three inequalities (Step 1 × Step 2 × Step 4):

$$
\Pr\bigl(\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} > \varepsilon\bigr) \;\leq\; 8\,(n + 1)^V\,\exp\!\bigl(-n\,\varepsilon^2 / 32\bigr).
$$

The right-hand side is summable in $n$ for every $V \geq 0$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$ (polynomial times exponential, with exponential winning). Borel–Cantelli delivers $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \leq \varepsilon$ eventually a.s.; since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \to 0$ a.s.

$\blacksquare$ — using VAP1971, SAU1972, vdV–Wellner 1996 Lem 2.3.7 (symmetrization), and the Hoeffding bound from Topic 12 §12.3.

</ProofBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={6} title="Halfspaces in $\mathbb{R}^d$ have VC dimension $d + 1$">

Consider $\mathcal{F} = \{\mathbf{1}\{w \cdot x \le b\} : w \in \mathbb{R}^d,\, b \in \mathbb{R}\}$, the class of halfspace indicators in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Take any $d + 1$ points in general position (no $d + 1$ on a common affine hyperplane) — these *shatter*: for any target labelling, solve the linear system $\mathrm{sign}(w \cdot x_i - b) = \pm 1$ coordinate-wise, and a non-degenerate Radon separation always exists. So $\mathrm{VC}(\mathcal{F}) \ge d + 1$.

The matching upper bound is Radon's theorem: any $d + 2$ points in $\mathbb{R}^d$ admit a partition into two subsets whose convex hulls intersect. Pick such a partition and target the labelling *+1 for one subset, −1 for the other*; a separating hyperplane would have to put both convex hulls on opposite sides, which is impossible because they share a point. No halfspace realises this labelling, so $d + 2$ points never shatter, and $\mathrm{VC}(\mathcal{F}) = d + 1$.

</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={7} title="Axis-aligned rectangles in $\mathbb{R}^2$ have VC dimension 4">

The class $\mathcal{F} = \{\mathbf{1}\{a_1 \le x_1 \le b_1, a_2 \le x_2 \le b_2\}\}$ of axis-aligned rectangles in $\mathbb{R}^2$ shatters the four-point diamond $\{(0, 1), (1, 0), (0, -1), (-1, 0)\}$: for any target labelling, put the tightest axis-aligned rectangle around the "+1"-labelled points — the four-point geometry guarantees no "−1" point lies inside. So $\mathrm{VC}(\mathcal{F}) \ge 4$.

Now take any $5$ points. Form the tightest axis-aligned bounding box around them. Each of the four sides of this box is "supported" by at least one of the five points (the side coincides with the extremal $x$- or $y$-coordinate); by pigeonhole at least one point is *not* extremal on any side. Label the non-extremal point $-1$ and the four extremal points $+1$. Any rectangle containing the four $+1$-points must contain their axis-aligned bounding box, which contains the non-extremal point — so it labels the $-1$-point as $+1$. No rectangle realises this labelling, and $\mathrm{VC}(\mathcal{F}) = 4$.

</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={8} title="ERM uniform-convergence bound (discharges LLN §10.7 Ex 8)">

Topic 10 §10.7 Ex 8 asked: *is Glivenko–Cantelli what makes ERM work?* and left the answer as a forward-pointer to "empirical-process theory (coming in the Statistical Inference track)". Thm 2 discharges that IOU. Concretely: fix a loss $\ell$ and a hypothesis class $\Theta$ such that the induced loss class $\{\ell(\cdot; \theta) : \theta \in \Theta\}$ has VC dimension $V$. Let $\hat\theta_n = \arg\min_\theta \hat R_n(\theta)$ and $\theta^\star = \arg\min_\theta R(\theta)$. Feeding Thm 2's high-probability bound through the ERM triangle inequality gives

$$
R(\hat\theta_n) - R(\theta^\star) \;\le\; 2 \sup_{\theta \in \Theta} |\hat R_n(\theta) - R(\theta)| \;\le\; O\!\Bigl(\sqrt{V \log n / n}\,\Bigr)
$$

with probability $\ge 1 - n^{-c}$ for any $c > 0$. The $\sqrt{V/n}$ scaling is the cleanest statement of ERM consistency the topic produces, and the Topic 10 IOU is paid in full.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={7} title="Proof-strategy overview">

The five-step proof above is a template, not a one-off. Symmetrisation → Rademacher randomisation → Sauer reduction → Hoeffding + union bound → Borel–Cantelli. Every VC-flavoured, PAC-flavoured, and Rademacher-complexity result in statistical learning theory reuses *at least three* of these five moves, usually in the same order. Once you've digested the Thm 2 proof, the PAC-Bayes bound is a symmetrisation + change-of-measure; Rademacher-complexity bounds are a symmetrisation + contraction; uniform-convergence-of-risk is this proof with a generic loss class instead of $\{0,1\}$-indicators. The Hoeffding step is the only one that genuinely requires "$f$ is bounded"; everything else works for general $\mathcal{F}$, with Hoeffding replaced by Bernstein or Talagrand (§32.9).

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={8} title="VC extensions — out of scope but named">

VC dimension is the binary-classification complexity measure; for multi-class problems the *Natarajan dimension* takes over, for real-valued regression classes it's the *pseudo-dimension* or *fat-shattering dimension*, and for kernel methods Rademacher complexity gives sharper data-dependent rates that VC cannot. Each refinement costs more machinery but delivers the uniform-convergence payoff for a broader hypothesis class. All three are deferred to formalML's [generalization-bounds](https://formalml.com/topics/generalization-bounds) topic, which carries the full menu. §32.4 stays at classical binary VC because it's the cleanest case and the one that carries the rest of the topic — the symmetrisation + Sauer combo is the reusable pattern.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={9} title="The fundamental theorem of statistical learning">

Thm 2 combined with Ex 8 is what Shalev-Shwartz and Ben-David (2014, Ch. 6) call *the fundamental theorem of statistical learning*: a binary hypothesis class $\Theta$ is *PAC-learnable* if and only if it has finite VC dimension, and the sample complexity of learning it is $\Theta(V / \varepsilon^2)$ up to log factors. The iff is striking — finite VC is both necessary and sufficient for uniform generalisation — and it's the cleanest structural result in statistical learning theory. Topic 32's most visible ML payoff is this theorem; everything else in the topic either builds toward it (§§32.1–32.3) or lifts it to richer settings (§§32.5–32.9). formalML picks up from here and develops neural-net-scale generalisation bounds where classical VC fails and Rademacher complexity takes over.

</RemarkBlock>

Donsker's theorem is the featured result of Topic 32 and the curriculum's single biggest forward-promise payoff. It says that $\sqrt n (F_n - F)$, viewed as a random function on $\mathbb{R}$, converges weakly in $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ to an $F$-indexed Brownian bridge $\mathbb{B}_F$. Topic 29 §29.8 Proof 3 took this as given to derive the Kolmogorov limit; here we pay the proof in full. Read the statement, then take a moment with Figure 4 — watching ten $\mathbb{G}_n$ paths at $n = 2000$ overlay the Brownian-bridge reference paths is the best intuition for what "weak convergence in a function space" looks like before the rest of the proof lands. Then we prove it in four steps: reduce to the uniform case via the PIT, establish finite-dimensional convergence via the multivariate CLT, upgrade to tightness via a bracketing-number bound, and combine.

<DefinitionBlock number={8} title="Standard Brownian bridge $\mathbb{B}^\circ$ and $F$-Brownian bridge $\mathbb{B}_F$">

A **standard Brownian bridge** $\mathbb{B}^\circ$ is a centred Gaussian process on $[0, 1]$ with covariance function

$$
\mathrm{Cov}\bigl(\mathbb{B}^\circ(s), \mathbb{B}^\circ(t)\bigr) \;=\; s \wedge t - s t, \qquad s, t \in [0, 1],
$$

and boundary conditions $\mathbb{B}^\circ(0) = \mathbb{B}^\circ(1) = 0$ almost surely. (The name "bridge" captures the latter: it's a Brownian motion pinned at both endpoints.) Given a continuous CDF $F$ on $\mathbb{R}$, the **$F$-Brownian bridge** is the process $\mathbb{B}_F : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by

$$
\mathbb{B}_F(t) \;:=\; \mathbb{B}^\circ(F(t)),
$$

a centred Gaussian process with covariance $\mathrm{Cov}(\mathbb{B}_F(s), \mathbb{B}_F(t)) = F(s \wedge t) - F(s)F(t)$. Both processes have continuous sample paths a.s. (Lem 3 below).

</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="lemma" number={3} title="Brownian-bridge construction (stated; BIL1999 §14)">

Let $W$ be a standard Brownian motion on $[0, 1]$. The process $\mathbb{B}^\circ(t) := W(t) - t\,W(1)$ is a standard Brownian bridge: centred Gaussian, covariance $s \wedge t - st$, and $\mathbb{B}^\circ(0) = \mathbb{B}^\circ(1) = 0$. Continuous sample paths on $[0, 1]$ a.s. are inherited from $W$'s continuity. Full construction and verification in BIL1999 §14. The `brownianBridgePath` helper in `nonparametric.ts` §17 implements the discretised version used by `EmpiricalProcessExplorer`'s reference overlay.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={3} title="Donsker's theorem (Donsker 1952; vdV2000 §19.1 Thm 19.5)">

Let $X_1, X_2, \dots$ be iid with continuous CDF $F$, and let $F_n$ denote the empirical CDF. Then

$$
\mathbb{G}_n \;:=\; \sqrt n\,(F_n - F) \;\rightsquigarrow\; \mathbb{B}_F \qquad \text{in } \ell^\infty(\mathbb{R}),
$$

where $\mathbb{B}_F := \mathbb{B}^\circ \circ F$ and $\mathbb{B}^\circ$ is a standard Brownian bridge on $[0, 1]$.

</TheoremBlock>

<ProofBlock number={2}>

The proof has four ingredients: **reduce to the uniform case** via the probability-integral transform, establish **finite-dimensional convergence** via the multivariate CLT, upgrade to weak convergence in $\ell^\infty$ via **tightness** — here, stochastic equicontinuity controlled by a bracketing-entropy bound on $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ — and finally **combine**.

**Step 1 — reduce to uniform.** Define $U_i := F(X_i)$ for $i = 1, 2, \dots$. Since $F$ is continuous, $U_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$, iid. Let $G_n$ denote the empirical CDF of $\{U_i\}_{i=1}^n$ on $[0, 1]$. For any $x \in \mathbb{R}$, $G_n(F(x)) = F_n(x)$, so the map $x \mapsto F(x)$ transports $(F_n - F)(x)$ to $(G_n - \mathrm{Id})(F(x))$. If we establish $\sqrt n\,(G_n - \mathrm{Id}) \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}^\circ$ in $\ell^\infty([0, 1])$, then composition with the continuous map $F$ gives $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}^\circ \circ F$ in $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ by the continuous mapping theorem (Topic 9 §9.5). Henceforth assume $F = \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$ and write $\mathbb{G}_n(t) := \sqrt n (G_n(t) - t)$ for $t \in [0, 1]$.

**Step 2 — finite-dimensional convergence.** Fix $k \geq 1$ and $0 \leq t_1 < t_2 < \dots < t_k \leq 1$. For each $n$, the vector $(\mathbb{G}_n(t_1), \dots, \mathbb{G}_n(t_k))$ equals $n^{-1/2} \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$, where $Y_i = (\mathbf{1}\{U_i \leq t_j\} - t_j)_{j=1}^k$. The $Y_i$ are iid, mean zero, with covariance $\Sigma_{jj'} = \mathrm{Cov}(\mathbf{1}\{U \leq t_j\}, \mathbf{1}\{U \leq t_{j'}\}) = t_{j \wedge j'} - t_j t_{j'}$. By the multivariate CLT (Topic 11 §11.5 Cor 2),

$$
\bigl(\mathbb{G}_n(t_1), \dots, \mathbb{G}_n(t_k)\bigr) \;\rightsquigarrow\; \mathcal{N}_k(0, \Sigma) \qquad \text{as } n \to \infty.
$$

The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is the covariance of $(\mathbb{B}^\circ(t_1), \dots, \mathbb{B}^\circ(t_k))$. Hence, the finite-dimensional distributions of $\mathbb{G}_n$ converge to those of $\mathbb{B}^\circ$.

**Step 3 — tightness via bracketing-integral control.** For weak convergence in $\ell^\infty([0, 1])$, finite-dim convergence combined with **asymptotic tightness** of $\{\mathbb{G}_n\}$ is necessary and sufficient (vdV–Wellner Thm 1.5.4). Asymptotic tightness for a random map in $\ell^\infty([0, 1])$ is equivalent, on a separable index set, to **stochastic equicontinuity**: for every $\eta, \varepsilon > 0$ there is $\delta > 0$ with

$$
\limsup_{n \to \infty} \Pr\!\Bigl(\sup_{|s - t| < \delta} |\mathbb{G}_n(s) - \mathbb{G}_n(t)| > \eta\Bigr) \;<\; \varepsilon.
$$

We establish stochastic equicontinuity by bounding the $L^2$-bracketing number of $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ and invoking the bracketing-integral equicontinuity inequality (vdV–Wellner Thm 2.5.6).

For the $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ class $\{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]} : t \in [0, 1]\}$ under $P = \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$: given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose a grid $0 = s_0 < s_1 < \dots < s_m = 1$ with $s_j - s_{j-1} \leq \varepsilon^2$ (so $m \leq \lceil \varepsilon^{-2} \rceil$). Define brackets $[\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, s_{j-1}]}, \mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, s_j]}]$; every $\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]}$ with $t \in [s_{j-1}, s_j]$ lies inside the $j$-th bracket. Each bracket has $L^2(P)$-width $\sqrt{s_j - s_{j-1}} \leq \varepsilon$. Hence

$$
N_{[\,]}\bigl(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}, L^2(P)\bigr) \;\leq\; \lceil \varepsilon^{-2} \rceil.
$$

The bracketing integral is therefore

$$
J_{[\,]}(1, \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}, L^2(P)) \;=\; \int_0^1 \sqrt{\log N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}, L^2(P))}\, d\varepsilon \;\leq\; \int_0^1 \sqrt{\log(\varepsilon^{-2} + 1)}\, d\varepsilon \;<\; \infty.
$$

Dudley's bracketing-integral equicontinuity inequality (vdV–Wellner Cor 2.5.2) yields, for every $\eta > 0$ and every $\delta > 0$,

$$
\Pr\!\Bigl(\sup_{|s - t| < \delta} |\mathbb{G}_n(s) - \mathbb{G}_n(t)| > \eta\Bigr) \;\leq\; \frac{C}{\eta}\,J_{[\,]}\!\bigl(\delta, \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}, L^2(P)\bigr)
$$

for a universal constant $C$ and all $n$ large enough. Since $J_{[\,]}(\delta, \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}, L^2(P)) \to 0$ as $\delta \to 0$, the equicontinuity condition holds. Hence $\{\mathbb{G}_n\}$ is asymptotically tight.

**Step 4 — combine.** Finite-dim convergence (Step 2) + asymptotic tightness (Step 3) $\Rightarrow$ $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}^\circ$ in $\ell^\infty([0, 1])$ (vdV–Wellner Thm 1.5.4). The limit $\mathbb{B}^\circ$ has continuous sample paths a.s. (Lem 3; BIL1999 §14), so convergence in $\ell^\infty([0, 1])$ coincides with convergence in $C([0, 1])$. Composition with the continuous $F$ (Step 1) transports to $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}^\circ \circ F = \mathbb{B}_F$ in $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R})$.

$\blacksquare$ — using DON1952, vdV–Wellner 1996 Thm 1.5.4 (finite-dim + tight ⇒ weak), Thm 2.5.6 (bracketing-integral equicontinuity), BIL1999 §14 (Brownian-bridge continuity), and Topic 11 §11.5 Cor 2 (multivariate CLT).

</ProofBlock>

**Corollary (Kolmogorov limit — discharges Topic 29 §29.8 IOU).** Applying the continuous sup-norm functional to both sides of Donsker's theorem yields

$$
\sqrt n \,\|F_n - F\|_\infty \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \sup_{t \in [0, 1]} |\mathbb{B}^\circ(t)| \;\sim\; K,
$$

where $K$ is the classical Kolmogorov distribution $\Pr(K \le x) = 1 - 2 \sum_{k \ge 1} (-1)^{k-1} e^{-2 k^2 x^2}$. Topic 29 §29.8 stated this limit with the Donsker step deferred; §32.5 Thm 3 now supplies that step, and the corollary is a one-line continuous-mapping application.

<ExampleBlock number={9} title="KS limit via CMT — recapitulates §29.8">

Apply the continuous mapping theorem (Topic 9 §9.5) with the sup-norm functional $\phi : \ell^\infty(\mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R},\, \phi(z) = \|z\|_\infty = \sup_{t \in \mathbb{R}} |z(t)|$, which is continuous in the $\ell^\infty$-topology. Thm 3 gives $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}_F$, so

$$
\sqrt n \,\|F_n - F\|_\infty \;=\; \phi(\mathbb{G}_n) \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \phi(\mathbb{B}_F) \;=\; \sup_{t \in \mathbb{R}} |\mathbb{B}_F(t)|.
$$

Since $\mathbb{B}_F(t) = \mathbb{B}^\circ(F(t))$ and $F$ is a continuous CDF onto $[0, 1]$, $\sup_t |\mathbb{B}_F(t)| = \sup_{u \in [0, 1]} |\mathbb{B}^\circ(u)|$, which by BIL1999 §12 has the Kolmogorov distribution $K$ with $\Pr(K \le x) = 1 - 2 \sum_{k \ge 1} (-1)^{k-1} e^{-2 k^2 x^2}$. This is the Kolmogorov limit Topic 29 §29.8 deferred — now paid via a one-line CMT application to the just-proven Donsker.

</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={10} title="Cramér–von-Mises as a continuous functional of $\mathbb{G}_n$">

Apply CMT with $\phi(z) = \int z(t)^2\, dF(t)$, a continuous $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}$ functional. Donsker gives

$$
n \int (F_n - F)^2\, dF \;=\; \phi(\mathbb{G}_n) \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \phi(\mathbb{B}_F) \;=\; \int_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{B}_F(t)^2\, dF(t) \;\stackrel{d}{=}\; \int_0^1 \mathbb{B}^\circ(u)^2\, du
$$

(the second equality by the substitution $u = F(t)$). The right-hand side is the integrated squared Brownian-bridge, which by a Karhunen–Loève eigen-expansion equals $\sum_{k \ge 1} \chi^2_{1, k} / (k \pi)^2$ with iid $\chi^2_1$ components. A weighted-$\chi^2$ limit — not Gaussian — despite every finite evaluation of $\mathbb{G}_n$ being asymptotically Gaussian. §32.8 builds the Cramér–von-Mises test on exactly this limit.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={10} title="Fidi + tightness: the two-ingredient structure">

vdV–Wellner Thm 1.5.4 turns weak convergence in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ into a pair of necessary-and-sufficient conditions: (i) finite-dimensional convergence of $(\mathbb{G}_n(f_1), \dots, \mathbb{G}_n(f_k))$ for every finite $\{f_1, \dots, f_k\} \subset \mathcal{F}$, and (ii) asymptotic tightness of the sequence in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$. Every empirical-process weak-convergence proof — ours, the bootstrap empirical process of §32.8 Thm 8, the local Donsker theorems of formalML's `semiparametric-inference` — factors the argument this way. The fidi piece is always "multivariate CLT"; the tight piece is always "some entropy bound + chaining". You'll see the pattern repeat.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={11} title="The Bahadur-linearization lift (explicit connection to §31.3)">

Topic 31 §31.3's bootstrap-consistency proof (Bickel–Freedman) had a three-step structure: scalar CLT ⇒ pointwise CDF convergence ⇒ Polya's theorem upgrades pointwise to uniform (Kolmogorov-distance). That is *exactly* the finite-dimensional shadow of the §32.5 Donsker proof you just read. Line up the three steps: §32.5 Step 2 (multivariate fidi CLT) is §31.3's scalar CLT lifted to $k$ dimensions; §32.5 Step 3 (bracketing-integral equicontinuity) is §31.3's Polya upgrade, lifted one level from "uniform in $x$" to "uniform over function-space neighbourhoods". The bootstrap is the Donsker-level resampling operation, restricted to a scalar functional. Naming this lift explicitly is the curriculum's most load-bearing structural retrospective — and §32.8 Thm 8 closes the loop by proving the bootstrap empirical process converges to the same Brownian-bridge limit as the original.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={12} title="Why continuous $F$">

Continuity of $F$ is load-bearing in Step 1: the probability-integral transform $U_i = F(X_i)$ is Uniform$(0, 1)$ only when $F$ has no atoms. When $F$ jumps at some point $t_0$ (e.g., a mixed discrete-continuous distribution), $F_n(t_0^-) \ne F_n(t_0)$ a.s. and the path $t \mapsto \mathbb{G}_n(t)$ has a non-vanishing jump at $t_0$ — it can't converge uniformly to a continuous-path limit. The fix is to replace $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ with the Skorokhod space $D[0, 1]$ of càdlàg functions and use its $J_1$-topology, which allows "small time perturbations" that absorb the jump. All of Topic 32 stays inside the continuous-$F$ case; the Skorokhod-topology generalisation is standard (BIL1999 §12–13) and deferred to formalML's `stochastic-processes` topics.

</RemarkBlock>

## 32.6 Metric entropy and bracketing numbers

{/* SCAFFOLD §32.6
  - Opening: how big can $\mathcal{F}$ be and still be Donsker? The entropy-integral criterion.
  - Def 9: covering number $N(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|)$.
  - Def 10: bracketing number $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|)$.
  - Def 11: bracketing integral $J_{[\,]}$, $P$-Donsker class.
  - Thm 4 (stated): Dudley 1978 — $J_{[\,]}(\infty, \mathcal{F}, L^2(P)) < \infty$ ⇒ $\mathcal{F}$ Donsker.
  - Fig 5 (32-bracketing-vs-covering): bracketing-number geometry + loglog-slope-2 verification.
  - Ex 11: bracketing number for Lipschitz functions.
  - Ex 12: Dudley's entropy integral worked for $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$.
  - Rem 13: covering vs bracketing — when they differ.
  - Rem 14: Bickel–Rosenblatt — simultaneous CIs for KDE (discharges Topic 30 §30.5 Rem 15).
  - Rem 15: entropy-rate sharpness — exact rates deferred to formalML.
*/}

§32.5 proved Donsker for one specific function class, $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$, by counting "brackets" — pairs of functions that sandwich every element of the class to within $\varepsilon$ in $L^2(P)$. That trick works for far more classes than $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ alone. Dudley's 1978 theorem says: if the bracketing numbers $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^2(P))$ grow slowly enough that $\int_0^1 \sqrt{\log N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^2(P))}\,d\varepsilon < \infty$, then $\mathcal{F}$ is Donsker. The integral is the *bracketing integral* $J_{[\,]}$, and it's the general Donsker criterion — §32.5 Step 3 was this condition, specialised to $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$, evaluated in closed form. This section names the abstract version and works two more worked examples.

<DefinitionBlock number={9} title="Covering number $N(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|)$">

Given $\mathcal{F}$ and a norm $\|\cdot\|$ on functions $\mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$, the **$\varepsilon$-covering number** $N(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|)$ is the smallest number of $\|\cdot\|$-balls of radius $\varepsilon$ needed to cover $\mathcal{F}$. Equivalently, it's the smallest $N$ such that $\mathcal{F}$ admits an $\varepsilon$-net $\{g_1, \dots, g_N\} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ with $\min_k \|f - g_k\| \le \varepsilon$ for every $f \in \mathcal{F}$. The **metric entropy** is $\log N(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|)$. For VC classes the $L^2(P)$-covering number satisfies $\log N(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^2(P)) = O(V \log(1/\varepsilon))$ (vdV–Wellner Thm 2.6.7).

</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={10} title="Bracketing number $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|)$">

A **bracket** $[\ell, u]$ is a pair of functions $\ell, u : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\ell(x) \le u(x)$ pointwise for every $x$. It *contains* $f \in \mathcal{F}$ if $\ell(x) \le f(x) \le u(x)$ pointwise. The bracket's **size** under $\|\cdot\|$ is $\|u - \ell\|$. The **$\varepsilon$-bracketing number** is

$$
N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|) \;:=\; \min\bigl\{N : \exists \text{ brackets } [\ell_1, u_1], \dots, [\ell_N, u_N] \text{ with size} \le \varepsilon \text{ covering } \mathcal{F}\bigr\}.
$$

Bracketing is strictly stronger than covering: a cover uses any $\varepsilon$-close functions, but a bracket enforces the *pointwise order* $\ell \le f \le u$. The result is that $N \le N_{[\,]}$ always, and $N_{[\,]}$ can be infinite for classes whose $N$ is finite — the extra rigidity matters. The bracketing number is what the Donsker criterion (Thm 4 below) actually needs.

</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={11} title="Bracketing integral and $P$-Donsker class">

The **bracketing integral** of $\mathcal{F}$ at scale $\delta$ under $\|\cdot\|$ is

$$
J_{[\,]}(\delta, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|) \;:=\; \int_0^\delta \sqrt{\log N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, \|\cdot\|)}\,d\varepsilon.
$$

A class $\mathcal{F}$ with a square-integrable envelope is **$P$-Donsker** if $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{G}_P$ in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$, where $\mathbb{G}_P$ is a tight Gaussian process on $\mathcal{F}$ with covariance $\mathrm{Cov}(\mathbb{G}_P(f), \mathbb{G}_P(g)) = P(fg) - (Pf)(Pg)$ — the *$P$-Brownian bridge indexed by $\mathcal{F}$*, generalising §32.5's $\mathbb{B}_F$ from $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ to arbitrary $\mathcal{F}$. Thm 4 gives the sufficient condition for Donsker-ness in terms of $J_{[\,]}$.

</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={4} title="Dudley 1978 — bracketing-integral sufficient condition (stated)">

Suppose $\mathcal{F}$ has a square-integrable envelope under $P$ and

$$
J_{[\,]}\bigl(\infty, \mathcal{F}, L^2(P)\bigr) \;=\; \int_0^\infty \sqrt{\log N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^2(P))}\,d\varepsilon \;<\; \infty.
$$

Then $\mathcal{F}$ is $P$-Donsker: $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{G}_P$ in $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$.

**Proof (deferred).** The argument is the general version of §32.5 Step 3 — symmetrisation + chaining against the bracketing-integral bound. Full proof in vdV–Wellner Thm 2.5.6 (~10 pages of careful measurability housekeeping plus three lemmas). We omit it because the §32.5 specialisation for $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ conveyed the essential structure; the general proof adds machinery but no new ideas.

</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={11} title="Bracketing number for Lipschitz functions on $[0, 1]$">

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be the class of $L$-Lipschitz functions on $[0, 1]$ bounded by $M$. Cover $[0, 1]$ with an $\varepsilon / L$-grid and bound each function on each grid-cell by a piecewise-constant upper/lower pair: the resulting brackets have $L^2(\mathrm{Leb})$ size $\le \varepsilon$, and there are at most $(M / \varepsilon)^{L/\varepsilon}$ of them, giving

$$
\log N_{[\,]}\bigl(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^2(\mathrm{Leb})\bigr) \;\lesssim\; (L / \varepsilon) \log(M / \varepsilon).
$$

Then $\sqrt{\log N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon)} \lesssim \varepsilon^{-1/2} \sqrt{\log(1/\varepsilon)}$, and $\int_0^1 \varepsilon^{-1/2} \sqrt{\log(1/\varepsilon)}\,d\varepsilon < \infty$ (gamma-function calculation). Thm 4 applies: Lipschitz-$[0, 1]$ classes are Donsker under Lebesgue. This is the nonparametric-regression analogue of §32.5 — replace "ECDF in $\mathbb{R}$" with "smoothed estimator of a Lipschitz regression function", same empirical-process machinery.

</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={12} title="Dudley's entropy integral worked for $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$">

§32.5 Step 3 computed $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}, L^2(P)) \le \lceil \varepsilon^{-2} \rceil$ for $P = \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$. Plugging into the bracketing integral:

$$
J_{[\,]}(1, \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}, L^2(P)) \;=\; \int_0^1 \sqrt{\log \lceil \varepsilon^{-2} \rceil}\,d\varepsilon \;\approx\; \int_0^1 \sqrt{2 \log(1/\varepsilon)}\,d\varepsilon \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{2} \;<\; \infty,
$$

using the standard Gaussian-tail integral $\int_0^1 \sqrt{\log(1/\varepsilon)}\,d\varepsilon = \sqrt{\pi}/2$. Finiteness confirmed — Thm 4 delivers §32.5 Thm 3 as a specialisation, which is exactly what we proved in full. The $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{cdf}}$ calculation is the cleanest worked example of Dudley's criterion.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={13} title="Covering vs bracketing — when they differ">

Covering and bracketing numbers agree up to polynomial factors for VC classes (vdV–Wellner Thm 2.6.7 + Thm 2.7.11), but for general classes they can diverge dramatically — the bracketing number enforces a pointwise sandwich that covering does not. Why does Donsker need the stronger version? Because the chaining argument in Thm 4's proof bounds the modulus of continuity of $\mathbb{G}_n$ using $\sup$ of differences, and sup-differences respect the bracketing structure (within a bracket, $|f - g| \le u - \ell$) but not the covering one (two close functions in $L^2$ can differ a lot pointwise). DUD1978 settled this — bracketing is the correct complexity measure for Donsker, covering for Glivenko–Cantelli.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={14} title="Bickel–Rosenblatt 1973 (discharges Topic 30 §30.5 Rem 15)">

Topic 30 §30.5 Rem 15 forward-promised simultaneous confidence bands for the kernel density estimator $\hat f_h$ as "Bickel–Rosenblatt-type functional-CLT machinery". The promised result is this: under regularity, an appropriately rescaled $\hat f_h - f$ satisfies a functional CLT in $\ell^\infty$-topology on a compact interval, with a Gaussian limit process whose covariance depends on the kernel and the bandwidth. The proof uses Thm 4 applied to the kernel-smoothed function class $\{K_h(\cdot - x) : x \in [a, b]\}$, whose bracketing integral is finite for Lipschitz kernels. Full statement and proof in formalML's `density-estimation` topic; naming it here discharges Topic 30's IOU and connects §32.6's abstract framework to a concrete ML-adjacent application.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={15} title="Entropy-rate sharpness deferred">

Thm 4 delivers Donsker but not the *rate* at which $\|\mathbb{G}_n\|_\mathcal{F}$ concentrates around its expectation — and the rate depends on the bracketing-entropy growth past the $J_{[\,]} < \infty$ threshold. Talagrand's sharp upper-lower-bound theory (TAL1996, GIN2016 Ch. 3) characterises exact rates in terms of metric-entropy integrals and generic-chaining functionals. For ML applications, the sharpness often matters: the gap between $\sqrt{V/n}$ and $\sqrt{V \log n / n}$ in generalisation bounds, for instance, is exactly this question. We defer the sharp-rate theory to formalML's `empirical-process-rates` topic; Topic 32 stays at the Donsker-or-not level, which is the cleanest structural story.

</RemarkBlock>

## 32.7 The functional delta method (★ full proof)

  - Ex 14: KS-distance functional $\phi(F) = \sup_t |F(t) - F_0(t)|$.
  - Rem 16: Fréchet vs Hadamard — why Hadamard is the right tangent-space notion.
  - Rem 17: influence function vs efficiency → formalML `semiparametric-inference`.
  - Rem 18: Topic 19 pivotal-CI connection (delta method underwrites Wald CIs).
*/}

So far we have $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}_F$ (§32.5) and, more generally, $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{G}_P$ for Donsker classes (§32.6). The natural follow-up question: if $\phi$ is a functional of the CDF — the median, the variance, a quantile, a Cramér–von-Mises distance — does $\sqrt n (\phi(F_n) - \phi(F))$ have a limit distribution, and is there a clean way to read it off? The answer, for "smooth" $\phi$, is the **functional delta method**: $\sqrt n (\phi(F_n) - \phi(F)) \rightsquigarrow \phi'_F(\mathbb{B}_F)$, where $\phi'_F$ is the Hadamard derivative of $\phi$ at $F$. This is the function-space generalisation of Topic 13's scalar delta method ($\sqrt n(g(\bar X_n) - g(\mu)) \to g'(\mu)\,\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$), with Hadamard-differentiability standing in for ordinary derivative — a looser notion that applies to far more statistical functionals than Fréchet differentiability.

<DefinitionBlock number={12} title="Hadamard differentiability tangential to $\mathbb{D}_0$">

Let $\mathbb{D}, \mathbb{E}$ be Banach spaces and $\mathbb{D}_0 \subseteq \mathbb{D}$ a subset (the "tangent set"). A map $\phi : \mathbb{D}_\phi \subseteq \mathbb{D} \to \mathbb{E}$ is **Hadamard-differentiable at $\theta \in \mathbb{D}_\phi$ tangentially to $\mathbb{D}_0$** if there exists a continuous linear map $\phi'_\theta : \mathbb{D}_0 \to \mathbb{E}$ (the **Hadamard derivative**) such that

$$
\frac{\phi(\theta + t_n h_n) - \phi(\theta)}{t_n} \;\to\; \phi'_\theta(h) \qquad \text{in } \mathbb{E}
$$

for every sequence $t_n \to 0$ in $\mathbb{R}$ and every $h_n \to h$ in $\mathbb{D}_0$ with $\theta + t_n h_n \in \mathbb{D}_\phi$ eventually. The "tangential" qualifier is the key relaxation: we only need the limit along sequences that stay in $\mathbb{D}_0$, which in practice is the subspace of *continuous* sample paths where $\mathbb{B}_F$ lives. Fréchet differentiability would require the limit along *all* sequences, which is too strong for most statistical functionals.

</DefinitionBlock>

<DefinitionBlock number={13} title="Influence function">

For a statistical functional $\phi : \mathbb{D}_\phi \to \mathbb{R}$ with $P \in \mathbb{D}_\phi$, the **influence function** at $P$ is

$$
\mathrm{IC}_P(x) \;:=\; \phi'_P(\delta_x - P), \qquad x \in \mathcal{X},
$$

i.e., the Hadamard derivative evaluated at the "point-mass perturbation" $\delta_x - P$. The influence function measures how $\phi$ reacts to a tiny bit of probability mass added at $x$ (and a tiny bit subtracted, uniformly, to preserve normalisation). By linearity of $\phi'_P$, $\sqrt n (\phi(P_n) - \phi(P))$ is approximately $n^{-1/2} \sum_i \mathrm{IC}_P(X_i)$, and its asymptotic variance is $P(\mathrm{IC}_P^2) = \mathrm{Var}_P(\mathrm{IC}_P)$. The influence function is the primitive notion behind efficient-influence-function estimators in semiparametric theory — see Rem 17.

</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={5} title="Functional delta method (vdV–Wellner §3.9; vdV2000 Thm 20.8)">

Let $\mathbb{D}, \mathbb{E}$ be Banach spaces and $\phi : \mathbb{D}_\phi \subseteq \mathbb{D} \to \mathbb{E}$ a map. Suppose $\phi$ is Hadamard-differentiable at $\theta \in \mathbb{D}_\phi$ tangentially to a subset $\mathbb{D}_0 \subseteq \mathbb{D}$, with derivative $\phi'_\theta : \mathbb{D}_0 \to \mathbb{E}$. Let $r_n \to \infty$ and $T_n : \Omega \to \mathbb{D}_\phi$ be random elements with $r_n(T_n - \theta) \rightsquigarrow T$ in $\mathbb{D}$, where $T$ is supported on $\mathbb{D}_0$. Then

$$
r_n\bigl(\phi(T_n) - \phi(\theta)\bigr) \;\rightsquigarrow\; \phi'_\theta(T) \qquad \text{in } \mathbb{E}.
$$

</TheoremBlock>

<ProofBlock number={3}>

The argument is Skorokhod representation + Hadamard differentiability in one line, wrapped in measurability housekeeping.

**Step 1 — transfer to almost-sure convergence.** By the Skorokhod representation theorem (vdV2000 Thm 18.9; applicable because $T$ is tight in $\mathbb{D}$), there exists a probability space $(\tilde \Omega, \tilde {\mathcal{F}}, \tilde \Pr)$ and random elements $\tilde T_n, \tilde T$ on it with $\tilde T_n \stackrel{d}{=} r_n(T_n - \theta)$ and $\tilde T \stackrel{d}{=} T$, such that $\tilde T_n \to \tilde T$ a.s. in $\mathbb{D}$. Since $\tilde T$ is supported on $\mathbb{D}_0$, outside a $\tilde \Pr$-null set we have $\tilde T_n \to \tilde T$ with $\tilde T \in \mathbb{D}_0$.

**Step 2 — apply Hadamard differentiability pointwise.** Define $\tilde T_n' := \theta + r_n^{-1} \tilde T_n$; by construction $\tilde T_n' \stackrel{d}{=} T_n$ and $\tilde T_n' \in \mathbb{D}_\phi$ eventually a.s. Applying the Hadamard-differentiability definition with $t_n = r_n^{-1} \to 0$ and $h_n = \tilde T_n \to \tilde T \in \mathbb{D}_0$:

$$
r_n\bigl(\phi(\tilde T_n') - \phi(\theta)\bigr) \;=\; \frac{\phi(\theta + r_n^{-1} \tilde T_n) - \phi(\theta)}{r_n^{-1}} \;\xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}\; \phi'_\theta(\tilde T).
$$

**Step 3 — translate back.** Since $\tilde T_n' \stackrel{d}{=} T_n$ and $\tilde T \stackrel{d}{=} T$, and since a.s. convergence implies weak convergence (vdV2000 §2.1), we have

$$
r_n\bigl(\phi(T_n) - \phi(\theta)\bigr) \;\rightsquigarrow\; \phi'_\theta(T) \qquad \text{in } \mathbb{E}.
$$

$\blacksquare$ — using vdV–Wellner 1996 Thm 3.9.4 and the Skorokhod representation theorem (vdV2000 Thm 18.9).

</ProofBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={6} title="Extended continuous mapping theorem (stated)">

Let $g_n, g : \mathbb{D} \to \mathbb{E}$ be maps with $g_n \to g$ uniformly on every compact subset of $\mathbb{D}$. Suppose $X_n \rightsquigarrow X$ in $\mathbb{D}$ with $X \in C_g$ a.s., where $C_g$ is the set of continuity points of $g$. Then $g_n(X_n) \rightsquigarrow g(X)$ in $\mathbb{E}$.

This strengthens Topic 9 §9.5's CMT in two ways: $g$ is replaced by a sequence $g_n$ (useful when the functional depends on $n$, e.g., studentisation), and the continuity requirement is only at the support of the limit. §32.8 Ex 15's two-sample-KS argument uses Thm 6 in its full strength. Proof in vdV–Wellner Thm 1.11.1; we take it as given.

</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={13} title="Quantile functional $\phi(F) = F^{-1}(p)$">

Take $\phi(F) = F^{-1}(p)$ for fixed $p \in (0, 1)$. At a continuous $F$ with positive density $f(\xi_p) > 0$ at its $p$-quantile $\xi_p = F^{-1}(p)$, $\phi$ is Hadamard-differentiable tangentially to $C(\mathbb{R})$ with derivative $\phi'_F(h) = -h(\xi_p) / f(\xi_p)$ (vdV–Wellner Lem 3.9.23). The influence function is

$$
\mathrm{IC}_F(x) \;=\; \phi'_F(\delta_x - F) \;=\; -\frac{\mathbf{1}\{x \le \xi_p\} - p}{f(\xi_p)} \;=\; \frac{p - \mathbf{1}\{x \le \xi_p\}}{f(\xi_p)}.
$$

Thm 5 gives $\sqrt n (\hat\xi_p - \xi_p) \rightsquigarrow \mathcal{N}(0, p(1-p) / f(\xi_p)^2)$ — Bahadur's representation theorem from Topic 29 §29.6, derived here in three lines by the functional delta method. The delta method is the right tool for this result; the Bahadur-representation proof of Topic 29 was the direct route for the median-specific case.

</ExampleBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={14} title="KS-distance functional $\phi(F) = \|F - F_0\|_\infty$">

Take $\phi(F) = \sup_t |F(t) - F_0(t)|$ for a fixed reference $F_0$. At $F \ne F_0$, $\phi$ is Hadamard-differentiable (the sup is attained at an isolated set of points), and Thm 5 delivers a Gaussian limit with an explicit influence function. At $F = F_0$, $\phi(F_0) = 0$ is the global minimum, and $\phi$ fails Hadamard differentiability — the sup-of-absolute-value kink at zero is an obstruction. Applying Thm 5 naively gives the wrong answer. The right answer comes from CMT directly: $\sqrt n (\phi(F_n) - \phi(F_0)) = \|\mathbb{G}_n\|_\infty \rightsquigarrow \|\mathbb{B}_{F_0}\|_\infty$, the Kolmogorov distribution. This is §32.5's corollary wearing a §32.7 hat — and illustrates the boundary of the delta method: at differentiability points you get Gaussian limits and efficient-influence-function machinery; at non-differentiability points, CMT on the underlying Donsker limit takes over and the limits are non-Gaussian.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={16} title="Fréchet vs Hadamard — tangential is the right notion">

Fréchet differentiability — uniform convergence of $(\phi(\theta + h) - \phi(\theta) - \phi'_\theta(h)) / \|h\|$ to 0 as $\|h\| \to 0$ over the whole ball — is too strong for most statistical functionals. The quantile, median, and KS-distance functionals are Hadamard-differentiable but NOT Fréchet-differentiable: the non-uniform dependence on the direction $h / \|h\|$ breaks Fréchet's uniformity. Hadamard's tangential version relaxes "all directions" to "tangent directions in $\mathbb{D}_0$", which for us is the subspace of uniformly continuous perturbations — exactly where $\mathbb{B}_F$ places its mass. This is why vdV–Wellner builds the functional delta method on Hadamard differentiability: it's the weakest smoothness notion that lets Thm 5 go through, and almost every statistical functional satisfies it.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={17} title="Influence function → semiparametric efficiency (formalML)">

The influence function of a functional $\phi$ is not just a technical object — it's the statistical primitive that modern efficient estimators are built from. An estimator $\hat\phi_n$ is **regular** if it's asymptotically Gaussian with asymptotic bias 0 along all one-parameter submodels through $P$, and the **Cramér–Rao bound** on its asymptotic variance equals $\inf_{\mathrm{IC}} P(\mathrm{IC}^2)$ over influence functions of $\phi$ in the semiparametric model. Efficient-influence-function estimators — targeted maximum likelihood (TMLE), augmented inverse-propensity weighting (AIPW) — explicitly estimate $\mathrm{IC}_P$ and plug it into a one-step correction to achieve the Cramér–Rao bound. formalML's [semiparametric-inference](https://formalml.com/topics/semiparametric-inference) and [causal-inference-methods](https://formalml.com/topics/causal-inference-methods) topics develop both in detail, both grounded in the Hadamard-differentiability framework established here.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={18} title="Topic 19 Wald-CI reconnection">

Topic 19 §19.2 constructed Wald confidence intervals $\hat\theta \pm z_{\alpha/2}\,\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\hat\theta)$ for parametric $\theta$, relying on the scalar delta method (Topic 13 §13.5) to pass a smooth transformation through the asymptotic normality of $\hat\theta$. Thm 5 is the function-space generalisation: sup-norm confidence *bands* for a nonparametric functional (e.g., $\|F_n - F\|_\infty$-based uniform bands, or $\phi(F_n) \pm$ a band derived from $\mathbb{B}_F$'s quantiles). Pivotal-CI constructions for functionals — the sup-norm Bayesian-credible-band analog on the frequentist side — use exactly this machinery, and §32.8 Ex 15 shows one worked example. The through-line from Topic 13 → Topic 19 → Topic 32 is: scalar delta ⇒ scalar Wald ⇒ functional delta ⇒ function-space confidence band.

</RemarkBlock>

## 32.8 Applications: Cramér–von-Mises, two-sample KS, bootstrap empirical process

{/* SCAFFOLD §32.8

§§32.5–32.7 built the machinery. §32.8 turns it on three concrete statistical objects: the Cramér–von-Mises goodness-of-fit statistic (a continuous functional of $\mathbb{G}_n$ with a non-Gaussian limit, via CMT), the two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov test (where two empirical processes collide and their difference inherits a Brownian-bridge limit after rescaling), and the bootstrap empirical process (Giné–Zinn 1990's functional upgrade of Topic 31's bootstrap consistency). Each is a direct consequence of Thms 3, 5, 6, or 7 applied to specific functionals or classes. If §§32.5–32.7 were the grammar, §32.8 is the vocabulary test.

<DefinitionBlock number={14} title="Two-sample empirical process">

Given independent samples $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim F$ and $Y_1, \dots, Y_m \sim G$, with empirical CDFs $F_n$ and $G_m$, the **two-sample empirical process** is

$$
\mathbb{G}_{n, m}(t) \;:=\; \sqrt{\frac{nm}{n + m}}\,(F_n(t) - G_m(t)).
$$

Under the null $H_0 : F = G = H$, as $n, m \to \infty$ with $n / (n + m) \to \lambda \in (0, 1)$, $\mathbb{G}_{n, m} \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}_H$ in $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R})$. The two-sample rescaling factor $\sqrt{nm/(n+m)}$ is the effective-sample-size version of the ordinary $\sqrt n$: for balanced samples ($n = m$) it simplifies to $\sqrt{n/2}$, and as $n \to \infty$ with $m$ fixed, it asymptotes to $\sqrt m$ — the bottleneck sample. The limit is the same Brownian bridge as in §32.5, so two-sample KS inherits Kolmogorov critical values verbatim.

</DefinitionBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={7} title="Cramér–von-Mises limit (stated)">

Under iid sampling from continuous $F$, the Cramér–von-Mises statistic $W^2_n := n \int (F_n - F)^2\,dF$ converges in distribution:

$$
W^2_n \;\xrightarrow{d}\; W^2 \;:=\; \int_0^1 \mathbb{B}^\circ(u)^2\,du \;\stackrel{d}{=}\; \sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{\chi^2_{1, k}}{(k \pi)^2},
$$

a weighted-$\chi^2$ distribution where $\chi^2_{1, k}$ are iid chi-squared-1 random variables. Proof: apply CMT to the continuous functional $\phi(z) = \int z^2\,dF$ on $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R})$; Donsker gives $\mathbb{G}_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}_F$, so $\phi(\mathbb{G}_n) = W^2_n \rightsquigarrow \phi(\mathbb{B}_F) = \int \mathbb{B}_F(t)^2 dF(t) = \int_0^1 \mathbb{B}^\circ(u)^2 du$ by substitution. The $\chi^2$ decomposition comes from a Karhunen–Loève expansion of $\mathbb{B}^\circ$. Critical values are tabulated; CvM is more sensitive than KS to tail discrepancies (the integrated-squared statistic emphasises bulk over extremes).

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock number={8} title="Bootstrap empirical process CLT (Giné–Zinn 1990, stated)">

Let $X^\ast_1, \dots, X^\ast_n$ be a bootstrap resample drawn iid from $F_n$ (Topic 31 §31.2), and let $F^\ast_n$ be its empirical CDF. Conditionally on the data $X_1, \dots, X_n$, the bootstrap empirical process is $\mathbb{G}^\ast_n := \sqrt n(F^\ast_n - F_n)$. Giné and Zinn (1990) prove:

$$
\mathbb{G}^\ast_n \;\rightsquigarrow\; \mathbb{B}_F \qquad \text{in } \ell^\infty(\mathbb{R}), \text{ conditionally on the data, in probability},
$$

meaning that for every bounded Lipschitz $h : \ell^\infty(\mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{E}^*[h(\mathbb{G}^\ast_n) \mid X_{1:n}] \to \mathbb{E} h(\mathbb{B}_F)$ in probability over the data. This is the functional analog of Topic 31 §31.3's scalar Bickel–Freedman consistency: whatever limit distribution the data-world $\mathbb{G}_n$ converges to, the bootstrap-world $\mathbb{G}^\ast_n$ converges to the same thing. Full proof in GIN2016 Ch. 3; we state it without deriving since the argument parallels §32.5 Thm 3 with an extra layer of conditional tightness.

</TheoremBlock>

<ExampleBlock number={15} title="Two-sample KS power via Anderson–Darling weighting">

The ordinary two-sample KS statistic $D_{n, m} = \sup_t |F_n(t) - G_m(t)|$ has balanced power across $t$, but alternatives that differ only in the tails — e.g., same mean and variance but different kurtosis — are hard to detect because the tail probability $F(t)(1 - F(t))$ is small precisely where the discrepancy lives. Anderson and Darling (1954) proposed the rescaled statistic

$$
A^2_{n, m} \;:=\; \sqrt{\frac{nm}{n + m}}\,\sup_t \frac{|F_n(t) - G_m(t)|}{\sqrt{\bar F(t)(1 - \bar F(t))}}
$$

($\bar F$ = pooled ECDF), which overweights the tails where the denominator shrinks. The limit distribution — via CMT on Donsker plus a rescaling — is $\sup_{u \in (0, 1)} |\mathbb{B}^\circ(u)| / \sqrt{u(1-u)}$, whose critical values are tabulated. For tail-discrepancy alternatives, $A^2_{n,m}$ has substantially higher power than $D_{n,m}$. The integrated version — the Anderson–Darling statistic $\int (F_n - \bar F)^2 / (\bar F(1 - \bar F))\,d\bar F$ — applies the same weighting to the CvM integral.

</ExampleBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={19} title="Bootstrap as finite-dim shadow of Donsker">

Topic 31 §31.3's bootstrap-consistency proof (CLT + Polya) was the scalar shadow of §32.5's Donsker (multivariate CLT + bracketing-based tightness). Thm 8 now makes the upgrade official: the bootstrap is not just a resampling heuristic that happens to work for the sample mean, it's a *functional* resampling operation on $F_n$ whose limit distribution is the same Brownian-bridge process Donsker delivers for the data. Every scalar functional $\phi$ you apply to $\mathbb{G}_n$ — mean, median, quantile, KS, CvM — has a bootstrap version you apply to $\mathbb{G}^\ast_n$, and the two asymptotically agree. This is the retrospective statement: the bootstrap, viewed from the empirical-process altitude, is just Donsker with data replaced by $F_n$. The §31.3 → §32.8 bridge is the curriculum's most important structural connection, and Thm 8 is its formal statement.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={20} title="U-processes and V-statistics — pointer to formalML">

A $U$-statistic of order $m$ averages a symmetric kernel $h$ over all $\binom{n}{m}$ subsets of the data; Topic 13 §13.3 introduced the scalar version. A *$U$-process* lets the kernel range over a class $\mathcal{H}$ of symmetric kernels and studies the resulting stochastic process $U_n : \mathcal{H} \to \mathbb{R}$. The limit theory — multi-level Donsker — involves Gaussian chaoses rather than a single Brownian bridge, and the technical machinery (Hoeffding decomposition + functional Donsker at each order) is nontrivial. $V$-statistics (plug-in vs. unbiased variants) follow the same theory with a different normalisation. Both are standard tools in kernel-method statistics and specific nonparametric tests; deferred to formalML's `u-processes` topic.

</RemarkBlock>

## 32.9 Concentration: Talagrand's inequality and symmetrisation

{/* SCAFFOLD §32.9
  - Opening: Donsker is about limits; concentration is about finite-sample tails.
  - Thm 9 (stated): Talagrand 1996 concentration inequality for empirical-process suprema.
  - Rem 21: the Talagrand proof is book-length (Ledoux–Talagrand 1991 Ch. 6–7).
  - Rem 22: M-estimator asymptotics / local empirical-process theory — forward-pointer to formalML `semiparametric-inference`.
*/}

Donsker's theorem gives the *limiting* distribution of $\mathbb{G}_n$; in finite samples, we often need tail bounds on $\|\mathbb{G}_n\|_\mathcal{F}$ that are explicit at every $n$, not just asymptotic. Classical Bernstein and Bennett inequalities handle bounded sums of iid random variables, but the supremum over $\mathcal{F}$ introduces a dependence structure Bernstein doesn't capture. Talagrand's 1996 concentration inequality fills the gap: it's the correct Bernstein-analog for empirical-process suprema, providing sub-Gaussian concentration around $\mathbb{E}\|\mathbb{G}_n\|_\mathcal{F}$ with explicit constants. It's the workhorse behind sharp generalisation bounds in ML and the sharp-rate theory §32.6 Rem 15 deferred.

<TheoremBlock number={9} title="Talagrand 1996 concentration inequality (stated)">

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a countable class of measurable functions on $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A})$ with envelope $F : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ satisfying $\|F\|_\infty \le M$ and $\sigma^2 := \sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} P f^2 < \infty$. Then for every $t \ge 0$,

$$
\Pr\!\Bigl(\|\mathbb{G}_n\|_\mathcal{F} \;\ge\; \mathbb{E}\|\mathbb{G}_n\|_\mathcal{F} + t\Bigr) \;\le\; K \exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{t^2}{C \sigma^2 + D\,t\,M / \sqrt n}\Bigr)
$$

for universal constants $K, C, D > 0$ independent of $n$ and $\mathcal{F}$. The inequality has two regimes: for small $t$ (the "sub-Gaussian" regime) the $\sigma^2$ term dominates and the bound is $\exp(-t^2 / (C\sigma^2))$; for large $t$ (the "sub-exponential" regime) the $tM/\sqrt n$ term takes over. Bousquet (2002) refined Talagrand's original constants to near-optimal values.

</TheoremBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={21} title="The Talagrand proof is book-length">

Honest warning: the Talagrand 1996 proof uses *isoperimetry in product spaces* — the most technically demanding machinery in modern probability theory — and runs to several chapters of Ledoux–Talagrand 1991 (Ch. 6–7 specifically; the preparatory material on entropy and Gaussian processes adds two more chapters). Reproducing the argument at Topic 32's pedagogical level would expand the chapter by 50% without adding conceptual payoff that the statement itself doesn't carry. We state the inequality, cite the sources, and use it; the full derivation is the kind of reference-book material formalStatistics deliberately doesn't reproduce.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={22} title="Local empirical-process theory — formalML pointer">

M-estimators (MLE, robust estimators, quantile regression, Z-estimators) have asymptotic-normality theory that specialises Donsker to neighbourhoods of the parameter value — "local empirical-process theory". The main result (vdV2000 §25 Thm 25.57): under local asymptotic equicontinuity of the score process and a non-singular information matrix, $\sqrt n (\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \rightsquigarrow$ Gaussian with the semiparametric-efficient variance. The proof uses Talagrand-type concentration on local empirical processes to control the remainder in the Taylor-expansion argument — exactly what Thm 9 gives. Semiparametric efficiency theory, targeted maximum likelihood, and all modern causal-inference estimators live on this foundation; the full development is deferred to formalML's [semiparametric-inference](https://formalml.com/topics/semiparametric-inference) topic.

</RemarkBlock>

<RemarkBlock number={23} title="Beyond $\ell^\infty$: Banach-space CLT">

$\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ is the simplest Banach space in which Donsker's theorem makes sense, because sup-norm convergence plays well with the bounded-function indexing and because chaining arguments reduce neatly to bracketing-integral bounds. General Banach-space CLTs — Donsker-like statements in Hilbert-valued or Banach-valued settings with abstract covariance operators — require Gaussianity assumptions on the limit and *type-2* geometric conditions on the space (Ledoux–Talagrand Ch. 10; Giné–Nickl Ch. 3). These generalise Topic 32's machinery to the high-dimensional-statistics setting where observations themselves live in Hilbert space (e.g., functional data analysis, kernel mean embeddings). All of this lives in formalML's `high-dimensional-statistics` and related topics; Topic 32 stays at $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ because it's the cleanest case and the one that carries the functional-delta machinery of §32.7 without additional geometric scaffolding.

</RemarkBlock>

## Curriculum retrospective

**What does shipping Topic 32 mean?** The site is now feature-complete at 32 of 32 topics. Every curriculum-graph node has `status: "published"`. Every forward-promise harvested from the 32-topic MDX chain has been discharged, either within formalStatistics or to a named formalML target. The formalStatistics-to-formalML handoff has six named targets: [`generalization-bounds`](https://formalml.com/topics/generalization-bounds), [`pac-bayes-bounds`](https://formalml.com/topics/pac-bayes-bounds), [`conformal-prediction`](https://formalml.com/topics/conformal-prediction), [`semiparametric-inference`](https://formalml.com/topics/semiparametric-inference), [`causal-inference-methods`](https://formalml.com/topics/causal-inference-methods), [`extreme-value-theory`](https://formalml.com/topics/extreme-value-theory).

**The five load-bearing theorems of formalStatistics.**

1. **Carathéodory's extension theorem** (Track 1, Topic 1 §1.6). The measure-theoretic bedrock: every countably-additive set function on a semialgebra extends uniquely to a measure. Everything downstream is a consequence.
2. **The Central Limit Theorem** (Track 3, Topic 11 §11.5, via Lindeberg–Feller). $\sqrt n (\bar X_n - \mu) \rightsquigarrow \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. The finite-dimensional template Donsker lifts one level.
3. **Rao–Blackwell + Lehmann–Scheffé** (Track 4, Topic 16 §16.7 + §16.10). Completeness + sufficiency + unbiasedness = UMVUE. The cleanest frequentist-optimality result in the curriculum.
4. **Posterior consistency** (Track 7, Topic 25 §25.8, via Schwartz's theorem). Bayes with a prior giving positive mass to every neighborhood of the truth recovers the truth in the limit. The Bayesian analog of consistency.
5. **Donsker's theorem** (Track 8, Topic 32 §32.5). $\sqrt n (F_n - F) \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{B}_F$ in $\ell^\infty(\mathbb{R})$. The curriculum closer: lifts LLN + CLT from pointwise/finite-dimensional to uniform/functional statements.

**Track-by-track recap.**

- **Track 1 (Foundations of Probability, Topics 1–4):** Kolmogorov axioms, conditional probability, random variables, expectation. The measure-theoretic primitives.
- **Track 2 (Core Distributions & Families, Topics 5–8):** discrete + continuous families, exponential families, multivariate distributions. The vocabulary for building models.
- **Track 3 (Convergence & Limit Theorems, Topics 9–12):** modes of convergence, LLN, CLT, large deviations. The asymptotic toolkit.
- **Track 4 (Statistical Estimation, Topics 13–16):** point estimation, MLE, MoM, sufficient statistics. The estimation framework.
- **Track 5 (Hypothesis Testing & Confidence, Topics 17–20):** NP paradigm, LR tests, CIs, multiple testing. The inference framework.
- **Track 6 (Regression & Linear Models, Topics 21–24):** OLS, GLMs, regularization, model selection. The regression framework.
- **Track 7 (Bayesian Statistics, Topics 25–28):** priors, MCMC, model comparison / BMA, hierarchical models. The Bayesian framework.
- **Track 8 (High-Dimensional & Nonparametric, Topics 29–32):** order statistics, KDE, bootstrap, empirical processes. The nonparametric framework and the track that closes the curriculum.

**The formalStatistics-to-formalML handoff, named explicitly.** The reader who has reached the end of Topic 32 is now equipped for:

- **Generalization bounds** — uniform convergence of training risk to test risk via function-class complexity, direct lift of §32.4.
- **PAC-Bayes bounds** — posterior-over-hypotheses generalization bounds, uses the symmetrisation technique of §32.4.
- **Conformal prediction** — distribution-free predictive intervals via exchangeability, uses the DKW inequality of Topic 29 §29.5 and the empirical-process framework of §32.
